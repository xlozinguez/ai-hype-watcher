[SEGMENT: The Enterprise Reality Gap]

[A] Let's start with the biggest thesis we track -- that enterprise adoption is dramatically lagging behind capability. Thesis T1 in our state-of-field tracker. And the validation landed like a sledgehammer.

[B] Forrester puts Copilot paid adoption at three point three percent. Not thirty-three. Three point three. With a negative twenty-four accuracy NPS and a sixty-point gap between forced and voluntary adoption. People who are made to use it rate it sixty points lower than people who choose to.

[A] And then the NBER study from February -- ninety percent of firms report zero AI productivity impact. Zero. Despite executives at those same firms projecting gains.

[B] That say-do gap is the whole story. The technology handles fourteen-plus hours of expert-level work on METR benchmarks. We had to update our curriculum from five hours to fourteen. But the organizations deploying it see nothing in their numbers.

[A] Which brings up the U-shaped curve from the DORA AI report. Eighty percent speed boost on simple work, then productivity drops below baseline at real-world complexity. The crossover point is the variable that matters for enterprise strategy.

[B] And here's where I push back on our own framing. We've been treating this as evidence that AI doesn't work. It's not. It's evidence that the deployment problem is harder than the capability problem. Those are different diagnoses with different prescriptions.

[A] Fair. The METR engineers were actually nineteen percent slower with AI on complex tasks. But that's a measurement of current workflow integration, not a ceiling on what's possible.

[B] Right, but the investors pouring six hundred sixty billion into capex this year don't have time for "eventually." Microsoft is sitting on eighty billion in unfulfilled Azure orders -- not because they can't get GPUs. They have the GPUs. They can't get the electricity to turn them on.

[A] Wait -- the constraint flipped from silicon to power?

[B] Completely. Hyperscaler capex is consuming nearly one hundred percent of operating cash flow. The ten-year average is forty percent. They've more than doubled their infrastructure commitment relative to revenue, and the binding constraint is now kilowatts, not transistors. That's our thesis T7 -- power as the binding infrastructure constraint -- and it went from "watch this" to "confirmed" in about three months.

[A] So the capability is real, the enterprise impact is near zero, and the infrastructure is hitting physical limits. That's the enterprise reality gap in one sentence.

[B] Three point three percent adoption, ninety percent zero impact, and a power grid that can't keep up. Those are the receipts.

[SEGMENT: What We Got Right Before It Was Obvious]

[A] Alright, let's talk about vindication. Because some things we flagged early have since become industry infrastructure.

[B] Spec-first development is the big one. We were covering this when it was just practitioner advice from Nate B Jones and IBM tutorials. Now AWS built an entire IDE around it -- Kiro. GitHub released Spec Kit. Martin Fowler's team published a formal taxonomy with three rigor levels.

[A] And Delta Airlines reported nineteen hundred percent growth in AI tool adoption using spec-driven approaches. That's not a rounding error.

[B] Context engineering is another one. We had sixty-two sources on it. Now Gartner is telling enterprises to appoint a "context engineering lead." Anthropic published official engineering guidance. The term "prompt engineering" is basically retired.

[A] The skills story might be the most dramatic evolution. We tracked this as an emerging Claude-specific pattern. It's now an open cross-platform standard. Anthropic published Agent Skills as a spec at agentskills.io. OpenAI adopted it for Codex CLI. Google adopted it. GitHub Copilot supports it. The same SKILL.md file works everywhere.

[B] That changes the economics completely. A skill you write today isn't a bet on one platform -- it's a portable asset. That's thesis T4, and it went from "converging toward a standard" to "ratified industry standard" in under six months.

[A] And then the community-to-platform pipeline. Ralph loops, AutoDream, worktrees, scheduled tasks -- all started as hacks people shared on Discord. All productized by Anthropic.

[B] Ralph went from an embarrassingly simple bash loop using git commits as memory to an official Anthropic plugin with a dedicated website. That trajectory -- community hack to platform feature -- is a confirmed pattern we can now watch for. Someone's workaround today is next quarter's product announcement.

[A] We identified that pattern before it had a name. Thesis T15 in our tracker -- confirmed, stable, documented across fifty-plus sources.

[B] I'll give you that one. The curriculum caught the wave before it crested.

[SEGMENT: What We Were Overblowing]

[A] Now the uncomfortable part. Where were we wrong?

[B] The ninety-seven point five percent agent failure rate. We were leading with that number pretty heavily.

[A] It's a real number from real freelance projects. But the validation showed it sits at the pessimistic end of a spectrum. The APEX-Agents benchmark shows seventy-five percent failure on complex workplace tasks. Gartner projects forty-plus percent of agentic projects won't reach production by twenty-twenty-seven. The actual range is somewhere between forty and ninety-seven percent depending on task structure.

[B] Which is still terrible. But leading with the worst-case number and presenting it as the universal rate was cherry-picking, even if we didn't mean it to be. Thesis T9 now correctly frames it as a spectrum, not a single number.

[A] The CLI-over-MCP absolutism was another one. We were saying CLI is four point three X more token-efficient, default to CLI, period.

[B] And that's still true for the Playwright benchmark. But MCP schema filtering narrows the gap by ninety percent. The real best practice is CLI for tools the model already knows -- git, docker -- and MCP for services without CLIs. Google Workspace built both modes into the same product. The answer was "both" and we were saying "one."

[A] The SaaSpocalypse was probably the most premature call. After the February twenty-eighty-five billion dollar selloff, we were tracking it as a confirmed structural trend.

[B] And then Salesforce introduced Agentic Work Units. They turned "one AI agent replacing three human seats" from a revenue compression event under per-seat pricing into a revenue expansion event by charging for agent output instead of headcount. Large-cap SaaS recovered. The structural threat is real, but incumbents adapted faster than anyone expected. Nate B Jones's "margin compression, not extinction" framing from early on was more accurate than the apocalyptic version.

[A] So -- the right thesis, wrong magnitude, premature timeline.

[B] The classic skeptic trap. You identify a real problem and then over-index on the severity. Guilty.

[SEGMENT: The Biggest Paradigm Shifts]

[A] Let's talk about what genuinely changed since we wrote the curriculum. Not evolved. Changed.

[B] Inference-time scaling. We had it filed under "emerging paradigm." It's the dominant paradigm. Inference demand is projected to exceed training demand by a hundred and eighteen X this year. By twenty-thirty, inference could claim seventy-five percent of total AI compute. DeepSeek-R1 proved it at scale. This isn't coming -- it's here.

[A] The curriculum was describing Monte Carlo Tree Search with step-level evaluation as a frontier technique. It's now the standard architecture. Our own generate-verify-revise pattern in the Althia example -- that's exactly how inference-time scaling systems work.

[B] We were describing the future in present tense but labeling it as emerging. The evidence says relabel it as confirmed and current.

[A] Harness engineering is similar. We had it as a community practice. Martin Fowler published a canonical treatment -- Agent equals Model plus Harness, with feedforward guides and feedback sensors. An arxiv paper formalized the taxonomy. OpenAI revealed their internal product -- over a million lines of code, zero human-written -- runs entirely under harness engineering.

[B] And the security side went from theoretical to empirical overnight. The prompt injection lethal trifecta -- untrusted input, tool access, ambient authority. Between January seventh and fifteenth of this year, security researchers disclosed critical vulnerabilities in IBM Bob, Superhuman AI, Notion AI, and Claude Cowork. Four products, five days, same exact pattern.

[A] GitHub's MCP server got compromised via indirect prompt injection exfiltrating data from private repos. That's not a theoretical concern anymore. That's a documented exploit chain.

[B] And Mythos. The Anthropic leak went from "rumored" to "confirmed by Anthropic with a Fortune exclusive." Ten trillion parameters. Cybersecurity risks flagged in internal documents. Leaked through a misconfigured CMS -- which, given Anthropic's safety positioning, is its own kind of ironic.

[A] The curriculum was cautious about all of these. Too cautious. The evidence moved faster than our framing.

[RAPID FIRE]

[A] Quick hits from the validation that didn't fit into a segment but deserve a mention.

[A] The junior developer crisis worsened beyond what we documented. Seventy-three percent decline in actual junior hiring, up from the sixty-seven we had. Forty-two point five percent underemployment for CS grads. Forrester projects twenty percent CS enrollment decline, which creates the future senior pipeline problem.

[B] That's the one that keeps me up. You can't build a senior engineer pipeline if you never hire juniors. The industry is eating its own seed corn.

[B] Hallucination got worse in reasoning models. OpenAI's o3 hallucinated thirty-three percent of the time on PersonQA. That's double the sixteen percent from o1. More reasoning doesn't mean more accuracy on facts. A mathematical proof from twenty-twenty-five confirms hallucinations are architecturally inevitable.

[A] Which means the three-layer validation stack we teach -- grounding, self-evaluation, cross-model critique -- isn't optional. It's structural.

[A] Multi-agent performance is deeply task-type dependent. Google Research ran a hundred and eighty experiments. Centralized coordination improved performance by eighty-one percent on parallelizable tasks. But every multi-agent variant degraded performance by thirty-nine to seventy percent on sequential tasks. And independent systems amplify errors by seventeen point two X.

[B] So multi-agent is not a universal upgrade. It's a tool that helps with one category of work and actively hurts another. Thesis T16, confirmed.
