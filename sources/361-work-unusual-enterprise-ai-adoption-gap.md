# 361: AI Isn’t Delivering (And CEOs Know It)

> **Creator**: Work. Unusual | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-22 | **Duration**: 13:25

## Summary

Despite relentless CEO optimism and massive investment, the first credible wave of enterprise AI data shows a striking gap between rhetoric and results. Consulting firms including PWC, Deloitte, Gartner, and McKinsey all report the same pattern: fewer than 12% of CEOs can point to measurable revenue growth or cost reduction from AI, less than 2% of companies have fully scaled a generative AI solution within even a single business function, and the number of companies reporting increased costs due to AI nearly offsets those reporting savings. The dominant mode across industries remains pilots, trials, and localized experiments—not transformation.

The video identifies four structural problems driving this gap. First, AI is a general-purpose tool (a "hammer"), not a targeted solution to a defined problem—unlike enterprise software purchases that map directly to a known pain point. Second, most employees lack the skills to translate AI capabilities into scalable process improvements; they optimize locally rather than restructure systemically. Third, bespoke, commercially proven enterprise AI solutions simply don't yet exist in most sectors—companies in industries like automotive R&D may have to build their own. Fourth, the dominant corporate focus on cost-cutting over revenue generation limits AI's transformative potential, since cost savings eventually equalize while innovation compounds.

The creator frames the current moment as broadly aspirational rather than transformational. Incumbent companies—GE, Boeing, GSK—have not restructured around AI; even the AI-native giants (Amazon, Google, Meta) are still in heavy investment mode, not reaping scaled cost benefits. The "Nachbarschaft" dynamic (wanting what the neighbor has without a deeper strategic rationale) explains much of current AI strategy: boards demanding AI roadmaps because AI seems important, not because specific business cases have been articulated.

## Key Concepts

### The Hammer Problem: Tool vs. Solution
AI in its current enterprise deployment behaves like a general-purpose tool rather than a purpose-built solution. Traditional enterprise software (Salesforce, CNC machines) is purchased because it solves a defined, measurable problem. AI requires companies to first identify the problem, then engineer the solution from a flexible substrate—a much harder organizational task. Until commercially proven, sector-specific agentic solutions emerge, most companies will continue improvising with a hammer.

### The Excel Trap: Localized vs. Scaled Solutions
Employees are adopting AI the way they adopted Excel—as a personal productivity tool for localized tasks—rather than the way cloud platforms were adopted, which delivered enterprise-wide scalability, security, and replicability. The analogy is precise: Excel's blessing (flexibility, ease of use) is also its curse (no corporate-wide insight or governance). Most current AI deployments share this profile: useful locally, impossible to scale or measure consistently.

### Measurement Vacuum
There is no established framework for consistently measuring AI's business impact. Companies know qualitatively that AI is helping—faster customer service responses, better PowerPoint slides—but cannot connect those improvements to revenue or quantified cost savings. This measurement gap is both a reporting problem and a strategic one: without clear ROI signals, companies cannot make rational resource allocation decisions or identify which pilots deserve scaling investment.

### Capability Overhang in Enterprise Adoption
The gap between what AI *can* do and what enterprises are actually capturing represents a classic capability overhang. CEOs believe AI will eventually be transformative, but the organizational infrastructure—change management skills, process redesign expertise, sector-specific enterprise tools—isn't in place. The result is scattered tactical initiatives driven by the *idea* of AI rather than clearly defined business cases, yielding unfocused strategy and weak operational prioritization.

### Cost-Cut Focus as a Strategic Ceiling
The dominant CEO use case for AI is headcount and cost reduction, which investors reward in the short term. But cost savings structurally equalize over time (rising electricity costs for data centers being one example), while revenue innovation compounds. The video argues that AI's transformative societal and economic potential requires an *additive* value-creation orientation, and current enterprise deployments are largely missing this. Even the model-builders (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.) haven't yet demonstrated how to turn massive infrastructure spend into durable profit.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Define the problem before deploying AI.** Teams that start with a specific, measurable business problem and work backward to an AI solution will outperform those who start with "use AI and be more efficient." The rat-poison/hammer distinction is operationally useful for scoping projects.
- **Treat measurement as a first-class deliverable.** Any AI initiative without a pre-defined measurement framework will struggle to prove value, attract further investment, or scale. Establishing baseline metrics before deployment is a prerequisite, not an afterthought.
- **Don't confuse individual productivity gains with organizational transformation.** If AI adoption in your company looks like people using ChatGPT to write emails faster, you are in Excel mode, not cloud mode. Scaled impact requires process redesign, not just tool adoption.
- **Expect sector-specific enterprise solutions to be the unlock.** For most non-digital-native companies, waiting for (or investing in building) a commercially proven, sector-specific agentic solution may be more valuable than continuous generic experimentation.
- **Reframe AI strategy around revenue creation, not just cost reduction.** Cost-focused AI strategies are easier to sell internally and to investors but hit a ceiling. Organizations that can identify AI-enabled revenue opportunities—new products, new customer experiences—will capture more durable value.
