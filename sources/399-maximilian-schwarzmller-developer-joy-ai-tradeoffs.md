# 399: It was more fun before AI

> **Creator**: Maximilian Schwarzmüller | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-26 | **Duration**: 12:20

## Summary

Maximilian Schwarzmüller, a prolific programming educator and active AI tools user, shares an honest personal reckoning with how AI-assisted development has changed his relationship with coding. Despite being deeply embedded in the AI tooling ecosystem — building projects with Claude Code, running agentic workflows on personal infrastructure, and publishing courses on AI coding tools — he articulates a genuine grief over what has been lost: the intrinsic pleasure of writing code, the flow state of problem-solving, and the craftsmanship of building software by hand.

His core tension is that AI has shifted his work toward the parts of software development he never enjoyed — writing specs, reviewing generated code, drafting test requirements, and steering AI agents — while eliminating the parts he loved. He frames this not as technophobia or resistance, but as an honest accounting from someone who has fully adopted AI tools and still finds the tradeoff depressing. He draws a sharp distinction between the productivity narrative ("AI makes you more productive") and the experiential reality ("AI has taken away what made this fun").

The video does not advocate abandoning AI tools — Max explicitly states there is no going back and that developers must learn to use them. Instead, it offers a counterweight to purely bullish AI content: an authentic voice acknowledging that technological capability and human fulfillment are not the same thing, and that the vibe coding era has real costs that productivity metrics don't capture.

---

## Key Concepts

### The Joy-Productivity Divergence
Max identifies a gap between AI's measurable productivity gains and the subjective experience of software development. He accepts that AI tools like Claude Code accelerate output, but argues that speed and productivity were never the full point — enjoyment, craft, and the intrinsic satisfaction of problem-solving were central to why many developers chose this work. AI optimizes for the output while degrading the experience of producing it.

### Work Shifting to the "Unfun" Parts
A concrete observation: AI has redistributed developer labor toward activities that many experienced developers find least rewarding — writing specifications, reviewing AI-generated code, steering agents, and explicitly dictating test coverage because models default to shallow happy-path tests. For someone who chose development precisely to avoid management-style oversight work, this reallocation feels like a loss of professional identity, not just a workflow change.

### The Vibe Coding Acceptance Gap
Max distinguishes between developers who find vibe coding liberating (those who care primarily about outcomes, not the act of coding) and those for whom the process was the point. He neither condemns nor endorses vibe coding but notes that the "future where you don't need to care about the code" is not appealing to everyone — and that framing it as inevitable progress erases legitimate preferences about how work should feel.

### Skill Atrophy as a Real Risk
He acknowledges AI's dual nature as a learning accelerant and a learning inhibitor. The same tool that lets you ask follow-up questions and explore concepts can also deliver answers without any understanding being transferred. He uses his own mouse-inversion script as an example of AI solving a problem he made no effort to understand — a pattern that scales dangerously if applied to core professional skills.

### Fundamentals as the Remaining Anchor
Despite his ambivalence, Max commits to continuing to teach and learn programming fundamentals, arguing they remain important for understanding and steering AI-generated code. This positions fundamentals not as resistance to AI but as the necessary foundation for responsible AI-assisted development — you need to understand code well enough to review what the model produces.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Spec and review discipline matters more, not less, under AI assistance** — because models default to minimal test coverage and happy-path solutions, developers must be more explicit upfront about requirements, edge cases, and test expectations than they would be writing code themselves.
- **Personal utility automation is a legitimate AI use case** — agentic tools running locally (e.g., a mouse-inversion script via a coding agent) can solve one-off problems without requiring the developer to deeply learn the underlying domain, freeing time for higher-priority work.
- **Acknowledge the experiential cost of AI adoption** — teams and educators should not assume that productivity gains translate to developer satisfaction; retention and motivation may be affected as the craft components of software work diminish.
- **AI enthusiasm and AI grief can coexist** — developers don't have to choose between embracing AI tools professionally and mourning what those tools have changed about their work; both responses are rational and honest.
- **Nobody knows the three-to-five year trajectory** — Max explicitly warns against anyone claiming certainty about how capable AI coding tools will be in a few years; the honest position is continued adoption combined with uncertainty about where the ceiling is.
