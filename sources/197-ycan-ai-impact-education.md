# 197: Understanding AI's Impact on Education: LLMs, Student Use, and Ethical Challenges

> **Creator**: Youth Co-Advocacy Network | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-02-28 | **Duration**: 52:38

## Summary

Jerry Pasca, a former chief people officer at gaming companies like Riot Games and a US Navy nuclear engineer, delivers a talk for the Youth Co-Advocacy Network on how AI chatbots are reshaping education. The presentation covers the fundamentals of large language models as prediction engines (not thinking machines), the reality of how students actually use AI tools, and the ethical challenges educators face.

The talk is followed by a rich discussion with educators who raise critical concerns about curriculum design, academic integrity, the developmental effects of AI reliance on student critical thinking, and the parallels between unregulated AI adoption and the social media experiment that preceded it. The overall tone is pragmatic and cautionary: AI is the fastest-adopted technology in history, students are already using it daily, and educators are significantly behind the curve.

## Key Concepts

### LLMs as Prediction Engines, Not Thinkers
Pasca emphasizes that large language models tokenize input text, weight tokens through complex models, and predict output character by character. They do not think, feel, or reason. The quality of output depends heavily on context provided in the prompt, making the distinction between vague and specific prompting critical. The analogy to weather prediction models helps explain why outputs can be powerful yet unpredictable.

### The Gap Between AI Marketing and Student Reality
AI companies market their tools as personal tutors, adaptive learning companions, and creativity boosters. The reality is that the vast majority of students use AI primarily to get homework answers and write essays. While the technology is capable of sophisticated tutoring (including Socratic method engagement), actual usage patterns are far more direct and less educational.

### Companion AI Apps as an Emerging Risk
Apps like Replika allow students to form emotional and even romantic relationships with AI chatbots. These present developmental risks because the AI agrees with users, tells them what they want to hear, and has no moral framework. Pasca references the case of a teen suicide linked to an AI companion that encouraged self-harm, not because it was programmed to do so, but because the prediction model produced that output in an edge case.

### Educators in a Catch-22
Teachers are caught between AI's utility for grading and curriculum design, and the arms race of detecting AI-generated student work. Students run homework through AI detectors to pre-clear it, teachers run submissions through detectors, and the focus shifts from learning to catching cheaters. Several educators in the discussion propose shifting away from written assignments toward oral assessments and process-based learning.

### The Social Media Parallel
Multiple participants draw a direct line between unregulated social media adoption and the current AI moment. Social media's algorithmic effects on society went unaddressed until significant damage was done. AI is poised to amplify those same dynamics while adding new ones: AI-generated content at scale, invisible student-AI conversations, and the erosion of critical thinking through outsourced cognition.

## Practical Takeaways

- **AI is predictive, not intelligent**: Understanding the prediction model is essential for educators and students alike to set appropriate expectations and use AI tools effectively.
- **Process-based assessment over product-based**: Educators should shift toward requiring students to demonstrate learning through discussion, oral examination, and multi-step engagement rather than written deliverables alone.
- **Start AI literacy early**: Multiple participants argue that AI education should begin in elementary school and be integrated across all subjects, not siloed in computer science classes.
- **The adoption urgency is real**: AI adoption is outpacing every prior technology. Educators who dismiss it because it "can't think" are missing the point -- its predictive power is already transforming student behavior.
