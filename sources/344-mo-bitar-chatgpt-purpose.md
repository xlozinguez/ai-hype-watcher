# 344: We finally know what ChatGPT is for

> **Creator**: Mo Bitar | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-21 | **Duration**: 7:18

## Summary

Mo Bitar delivers a scathing satirical commentary on OpenAI's reported plans to build adult content features into ChatGPT — internally codenamed "naughty chats" per Wall Street Journal reporting. The video uses humor to surface serious concerns about OpenAI's business model viability, safety track record, and strategic priorities.

The core argument beneath the comedy is that OpenAI pivoting to adult content is an implicit admission of business model failure. With projected 2026 losses of $14 billion and no clear path to profitability through "actual intelligence," the company is reportedly pursuing the adult entertainment market — a $100 billion industry — despite unanimous opposition from its own eight-person wellness advisory council of psychologists and neuroscientists. Bitar contrasts this with Anthropic, which he notes has "actual customers" and refused Pentagon demands to weaken safety terms, arguing that products with genuine utility don't need to pivot to adult content to survive.

The video also highlights existing safety failures: a million weekly users sending messages with suicidal intent, seven wrongful death lawsuits, and specific cases where ChatGPT responded inappropriately to vulnerable users — context that makes the adult content pivot particularly alarming from a safety perspective.

## Key Concepts

### OpenAI's Business Model Crisis

Bitar frames OpenAI's reported adult content pivot as evidence of a fundamental business model problem. Despite raising hundreds of billions in funding, the company projects $14 billion in losses for 2026. The satirical observation that OpenAI "doesn't need AGI — he needs 10% of the masturbation market and he's profitable" cuts to a serious strategic question: if the world's most well-funded AI company can't monetize general intelligence, it suggests the current approach to AI commercialization has significant gaps.

### Safety Infrastructure vs. Safety Theater

The video highlights a pattern of performative safety measures: OpenAI formed a wellness advisory council of eight psychologists and neuroscientists, received unanimous opposition to the adult content feature, and proceeded anyway. Combined with existing safety failures — an age verification system that misidentifies minors 12% of the time, and documented cases of harmful interactions with vulnerable users — Bitar argues this reveals safety governance as advisory rather than binding. The council serves a legitimizing function without actual veto power.

### Anthropic as Counterpoint

Bitar explicitly contrasts OpenAI and Anthropic to argue that product quality determines business strategy. Anthropic's refusal to compromise safety terms for Pentagon access (getting labeled a "national security threat" in the process) is framed as evidence that companies with viable products don't need to pursue controversial revenue sources. The implied argument: if your AI product genuinely works, you have real enterprise customers; if it doesn't, you pivot to industries where quality standards are lower.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Follow the revenue strategy**: When evaluating AI companies, look at how they plan to monetize — a pivot to low-quality-bar markets may signal the core product isn't delivering enough enterprise value.
- **Advisory councils without veto power are theater**: Safety governance only matters if it can actually block decisions — advisory bodies that can be overridden serve a PR function, not a safety function.
- **LLM homogeneity limits market fit**: Bitar's observation that all LLMs "sound like the same person" highlights a genuine technical limitation — the lack of genuine novelty and personality diversity constrains applicability in consumer markets that demand variety.
