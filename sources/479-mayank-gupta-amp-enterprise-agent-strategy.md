# Amp Code Founder on Future of Coding Agents

## Summary

Quinn Slack, co-founder of Sourcegraph and the AMP coding agent, traces his path from early engineering roles at Bleacher Report and Palanteer to founding Sourcegraph in 2013 with code search as the wedge toward eventual development automation. The interview reveals how Sourcegraph's enterprise customer base (four of six top US banks, nine of ten top public software companies) gave them an unusually clear view of enterprise AI adoption dynamics—including the painful lesson that customers who praised their security compliance work were simultaneously choosing Cursor instead.

That experience radicalized their product philosophy. AMP was deliberately spun off as a 20-person profitable team with no salespeople, no roadmap commitments, and no enterprise checkbox-ticking. The explicit strategy is to serve only the 15–20% of developers within a given company who want frontier capability at frontier cost, rather than building a watered-down product that can be deployed to 100% of a company's engineers. Quinn argues that trying to capture the full market would require stabilizing the product in ways that would cause it to fall behind—a fatal tradeoff in a space where the underlying models change continuously.

The conversation also covers Quinn's view that traditional packaged software is structurally dead, that coding agents as a category label is increasingly obsolete as the capability becomes ambient, and that AI's most profound impact on developers is eliminating the intermediaries between builders and their end users—restoring the tight feedback loop between coder and customer that he identifies as the core of great software development.

---

## Key Concepts

### Frontier-Only Positioning as Enterprise Strategy

AMP explicitly does not try to serve all developers within an enterprise. Quinn recommends that large banks use GitHub Copilot for the majority of their developers, while AMP serves only the 15–20% who want maximum capability and are willing to absorb unpredictable token-based costs. The logic is that building for 100% adoption requires feature and cost compromises that would make the product fall behind competitors who remain unconstrained. This is a direct inversion of typical enterprise SaaS land-and-expand strategy.

### The Gap Between Enterprise Rhetoric and Buying Behavior

Sourcegraph's Cody product exposed a structural trap: enterprise customers would spend nine months praising a vendor's security partnership, complete every compliance checkbox, and then choose a competitor anyway. Quinn frames this as customers saying one thing (we need all these enterprise controls) and doing another (we'll pay for the best product regardless). The lesson is that optimizing for stated procurement criteria rather than product quality is a losing strategy in fast-moving AI markets.

### Coding Agents as Transitional Label

Quinn made a provocative claim that "coding agents are dead" before clarifying the intent: the category label is becoming obsolete because agentic coding capability is becoming the baseline expectation rather than a distinct product tier. The underlying point is that the framing of "agent" is a temporary scaffold during a transition period, not a durable product category.

### Small Team + Profitability as Speed Advantage

AMP runs as a 20-person team spun out from Sourcegraph, self-service only, currently profitable. Quinn explicitly frames the small size as a competitive moat against both larger incumbents and the AI labs themselves: "We can change way faster than any of the labs." The argument is that in a market where the underlying technology changes continuously, organizational speed is the primary durable advantage.

### AI Eliminating Builder-Customer Intermediaries

Quinn's deepest claim about AI's impact is structural: most software engineers today are separated from their end users by layers of product managers, project managers, and process. AI coding tools promise to compress that stack, giving more developers the tight feedback loop between building and customer reaction that he associates with exceptional outcomes. This is framed not as job destruction but as a restoration of craft.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Don't try to serve all user segments equally.** Identify the subset of users who genuinely want frontier capability and design exclusively for them; building for median adoption in an enterprise context produces a product that loses to more focused competitors.
- **Distinguish customer stated needs from purchasing behavior.** Enterprise procurement criteria (security, compliance, price predictability) are necessary but not sufficient—actual purchasing decisions in fast-moving AI markets increasingly reward product quality over checkbox completion.
- **Small, profitable, self-service structures outmaneuver large sales-driven organizations** when the product surface changes faster than a sales cycle. Removing salespeople and roadmap commitments is a feature, not a limitation, in this environment.
- **Token-based variable pricing** (charging pass-through on model costs rather than fixed seat prices) is a viable model for frontier AI products—it signals commitment to always using the best models without being constrained by margin protection on a fixed price.
- **Traditional packaged software companies face structural obsolescence.** The model of building software once and licensing it broadly is under pressure from AI-generated custom solutions; builders should orient toward services and outcomes rather than static product licenses.
