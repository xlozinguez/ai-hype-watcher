# 278: Microslop Wants Your Medical Records Now...

> **Creator**: SomeOrdinaryGamers | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-13 | **Duration**: 18:29

## Summary

SomeOrdinaryGamers (Mudahar) covers Microsoft's announcement of Copilot Health, a product that aggregates users' medical records, wearable data, and health history to deliver "personalized health insights" via an LLM. The video is primarily a critical response to the product launch, arguing that sharing private medical information with AI companies is both a privacy risk and a medically dangerous practice given the fundamental limitations of large language models.

The creator draws on a personal near-death experience (a perforated appendix) to illustrate how even highly trained medical professionals required multiple days, multiple tests, and physical examination before arriving at a correct diagnosis. His core argument is that LLMs are probability engines trained to produce coherent text, not sentient reasoners, and that marketing them as "medical super intelligence" to the general public is dangerous hype. He also briefly connects this to broader concerns about AI being used in high-stakes decisions, such as military targeting systems, where hallucination has potentially lethal consequences.

The video also touches on vibe-coding and AI-generated code as a parallel concern: just as people deploy AI-written code without understanding its security implications, people are now being conditioned to hand over sensitive medical data without understanding how those companies may use or protect it. The creator's conclusion is that the primary driver of products like Copilot Health is not genuine utility but rather the AI industry's need to find commercially viable applications that justify the hype cycle.

## Key Concepts

### LLMs as Probability Engines, Not Reasoners
Mudahar repeatedly emphasizes that large language models are pattern-matching systems trained to generate statistically probable text, not systems capable of genuine reasoning or diagnosis. He cites ongoing hallucination problems as evidence that the industry has not crossed the threshold needed for trusted medical decision-making. This distinction matters because marketing language ("medical super intelligence," AGI, ASI) actively misleads users into misplacing trust.

### Privacy Risk of Medical Data with AI Companies
The video argues that no AI company's privacy guarantees are independently verifiable by users. Terms of service can be changed unilaterally, data may be routed through back-end systems users cannot inspect, and the structural incentives of data-centric tech companies are misaligned with user privacy. Conditioning users to voluntarily surrender their most sensitive personal data is framed as a long-term risk that outweighs any near-term convenience.

### AI Hype Driving Product Expansion into High-Stakes Domains
Copilot Health is presented as a case study in AI companies seeking tangible commercial applications to justify inflated valuations and hype narratives. Rather than genuine medical utility driving the product, the creator argues the causality runs the other direction: the need to monetize AI infrastructure is pushing these tools into domains (medicine, law, finance) where the cost of failure is borne by users, not companies.

### Hallucination Risk in High-Stakes Decisions
The video connects medical AI hallucination to a broader pattern of AI being applied in life-or-death contexts without adequate human verification — referencing allegations around AI-assisted military targeting (Palantir) where acting on bad model output is irreversible. Unlike debugging code, a misdiagnosis or a bombed school cannot be undone with a backspace.

### Misframing of LLM Capabilities by Users and Vendors
A practical example from a physician on social media illustrates how patients misuse AI: a patient asked ChatGPT about side effects of a nasal steroid and received information about systemic glucocorticoids instead, trusted the wrong answer, and suffered for a year. The problem is compounded because users "don't know enough to properly frame a question" — a limitation that accuracy benchmarks published by AI companies do not capture.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Do not upload medical records or sensitive health data to AI platforms**, regardless of stated privacy protections — there is no user-side mechanism to verify compliance, and terms of service can change at any time.
- **Treat LLM medical output as unverified general information**, not personalized medical advice — the system cannot replicate physical examination, lab work, or the clinical reasoning that catches edge cases.
- **Apply the same skepticism to AI "accuracy" claims in medical contexts** as you would to any vendor benchmark — real-world accuracy depends heavily on how well users frame questions, which is itself a medical knowledge problem.
- **Recognize "medical super intelligence" and similar terms as marketing language**, not technical descriptors — current models still hallucinate regularly and are not approaching AGI-level reliability.
- **The vibe-coding parallel applies here**: just as AI-generated code can introduce security vulnerabilities that non-technical users cannot detect, AI-generated medical insights can introduce dangerous errors that medically untrained users cannot catch.
