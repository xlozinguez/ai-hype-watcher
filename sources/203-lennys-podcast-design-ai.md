# 203: How AI Is Transforming the Design Process at Anthropic

> **Creator**: Lenny's Podcast (Jenny Wen) | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-01 | **Duration**: 77:25

## Summary

Jenny Wen, head of design for Claude Co-work (formerly head of design for Claude, previously director of design at Figma), discusses how AI is fundamentally reshaping the design profession. Her central thesis: the traditional design process — discovery, diverge, converge, iterate — is effectively dead, not because designers chose to abandon it, but because engineering velocity has forced the change. When engineers can spin up seven Claude agents simultaneously, designers can no longer be gatekeepers who produce beautiful mocks before anything gets built.

Wen describes two emerging modes of design work: execution support (consulting with engineers as they ship, polishing implementations, doing "last mile" work in code) and short-horizon vision setting (3-6 month prototypes that point teams in a direction, replacing the 2-5 year vision decks of the past). Her time allocation has shifted dramatically — from 60-70% mocking/prototyping to 30-40%, with the freed time going to direct engineer pairing and writing code herself.

The conversation also covers hiring philosophy (three archetypes: strong generalists, deep specialists, and "crack new grads"), the Co-work product development story (not actually built in 10 days — there were months of internal prototyping), and why chat as an interface isn't going away. Wen advocates "building trust through speed" — shipping early with caveats, then iterating visibly based on user feedback.

## Key Concepts

### The Design Process Is Dead (and Engineering Killed It)

The traditional design process (research, diverge, converge, mock, iterate) has been overtaken by engineering velocity. Engineers using agentic coding tools can prototype and ship faster than designers can mock. The result isn't that design is less important — it's that the designer's role shifts from gatekeeper ("here's the design, now build it") to consultant and collaborator who helps engineers ship cohesive, quality products at speed. Wen notes this isn't confined to AI companies; it's resonating across the industry.

### Two Modes of Design Work

Design is stratifying into two distinct activities: (1) **Execution support** — working alongside engineers, giving feedback, polishing implementations, doing last-mile CSS and UI work directly in code; and (2) **Vision and direction** — creating short-horizon (3-6 month) prototypes and directions that align multiple teams, replacing the long-arc vision decks that are impossible when the technology landscape changes every few months.

### Building Trust Through Speed

Rather than waiting to ship polished products, Wen advocates releasing early (labeled as "research preview"), committing publicly to iterate based on feedback, and then visibly shipping improvements. The brand degrades only when you release early and then nothing happens. Trust is built by demonstrating responsiveness, not by withholding until perfect.

### The Illegibility Framework for Design

Borrowed from VC Evan Tana, Wen applies the "legibility framework" to internal product development. Designers should seek out illegible ideas — internal prototypes with energy and excitement around them that don't yet make sense — and work to make them legible through UX and storytelling. The Claude Co-work skills framework emerged from exactly this pattern: an internal prototype ("Claude Studio") that was dense and confusing but clearly had something powerful, which designers helped distill into a shippable form.

### AI Taste and Judgment Are Improving

Wen cautions against over-investing in the belief that human taste and judgment are permanently safe from AI. While she believes humans will remain accountable for decisions (someone has to decide what gets built), she notes that AI's design sense is improving rapidly and that designers may be "holding on a little too much" to the idea that AI can never match human aesthetic judgment.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Designers should learn coding tools**: Not necessarily React from scratch, but tools like Claude Code in VS Code for last-mile polish and prototyping — this is becoming part of the core toolkit
- **Shift from gatekeeper to consultant**: Explain design principles to engineers rather than dictating designs; equip teams with design systems and reasoning they can apply independently
- **Figma still matters**: For exploring 8-10 different directions simultaneously, which agentic coding tools don't support well due to their linear, invest-in-one-direction nature
- **Hire for adaptability**: The three archetypes that succeed in this environment are strong generalists (block-shaped, 80th percentile in multiple skills), deep specialists (top 10% in one area), and eager new grads with blank slates
- **Management requires IC rotation**: Design managers need to periodically return to IC work to understand how the process has changed; managing without hands-on experience in the new tools leads to disconnection
