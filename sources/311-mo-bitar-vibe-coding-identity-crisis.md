# I was a 10x engineer. Now I'm useless.

## Summary

Mo Bitar offers a raw, personal account of how heavy AI coding tool usage has degraded his ability and desire to code independently. After successfully using Codex (GPT-4.5) to autonomously deploy his product Shape.work — giving the agent access to AWS CLI and GitHub API with minimal oversight — he finds himself unable to emotionally connect to the result. The code works, but because he didn't struggle to create it, he has no passion to sell or iterate on it.

The core tension Bitar articulates is that the craft of software development was never purely instrumental — it was transformative. Developers grew as people by wrestling with hard problems, sleeping on them, and waking up with solutions. That cycle of struggle and insight is what made them capable of building businesses and caring about their users. AI removes that struggle entirely, and with it, the psychological scaffolding that made independent software creation meaningful. He compares the output to a hot dog: functional, caloric, but soulless.

Bitar draws a stark analogy between AI tool dependence and addiction: once you experience the speed of AI doing a laborious task in 10 minutes that would take you a day, your brain reframes the slower manual approach as irrational. He concludes that there's no moderate path — no healthy balance between AI-assisted and hand-coded work — because the drug logic always wins. His proposed solution, going cold turkey, is one he admits he's probably unwilling to take because his livelihood depends on shipping fast.

## Key Concepts

### Vibe Coding as Evolutionary Process (Without the Craftsman)
Bitar extends a metaphor he introduced in a prior video: vibe coding resembles biological evolution — not directed, not comprehensible, but producing working results as long as outputs pass tests. Evolution doesn't understand hands; it just accumulates DNA that survives selection pressure. Similarly, an LLM doesn't understand your codebase, but if it passes your tests, you ship it. The problem isn't whether this works mechanically — it does — but whether the human in the loop retains any meaningful role or identity in the process.

### The Emotional Economy of Craftsmanship
Software built by hand carries what Bitar calls an "artisanal" quality — not necessarily in code elegance, but in the developer's relationship to the work. That relationship is what enables passionate selling, tight user feedback loops, and genuine product care. AI-generated software severs this bond. The creator has no emotional stake because they contributed no effort, faced no uncertainty, and solved no problems. This makes the output difficult to sell and the work impossible to sustain psychologically.

### Skill Atrophy and the Dependency Trap
Bitar describes a concrete mechanism of skill erosion: using AI for the "easy" parts erodes the cognitive habit of solving problems manually. The brain rapidly re-categorizes manual effort as irrational relative to the AI shortcut. Once that reframing occurs, returning to hand-coding feels not just slow but pointless — an internal resistance the developer cannot override through willpower. This mirrors addiction dynamics: the rational case for moderation collapses under behavioral pressure.

### The Code Review Problem at AI Speed
Responsible development practice assumes developers review their code. But AI agents generate code at rates humans cannot meaningfully review — the author cites this as an unresolved structural problem in agentic workflows. The OpenClaw example (Peter Steinberger never reading the codebase) is framed not as best practice but as proof that unreviewed AI code can ship at scale — raising questions about quality, security, and accountability that the industry has not resolved.

### Professional Identity Crisis for Developers
Bitar questions what developer identity, job interviews, and career capital mean in a world where manual coding speed is no longer a differentiator. The historic developer advantage — always employable, able to take sabbaticals and return to higher pay — may be eroding. The video captures a broader liminal anxiety: developers aren't sure whether adopting AI tools is adaptive or self-defeating, and both camps (adopt fast vs. resist) hold heated positions without clear evidence.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Full agentic autonomy is achievable but costly to identity**: Giving an AI agent access to cloud CLIs, GitHub APIs, and deployment pipelines can take a product from localhost to production in one session — but you will not understand what was built or feel ownership over it.
- **There may be no stable "hybrid" mode**: Using AI for tedious tasks while reserving hard problems for yourself sounds reasonable but behaviorally collapses — the speed differential makes manual effort feel irrational and the AI habit expands to fill available work.
- **Emotional attachment to code is not sentimental — it's functional**: Passion for the work is what enables founders to sell, support, and iterate on products. Removing the struggle removes the attachment, which removes the commercial drive. This is a practical business problem, not just a psychological one.
- **Test-based agentic loops are the closest analog to quality control**: If you're going to ship without reviewing code, defining clear validation criteria (does the app deploy? can users sign up?) is the only available selection pressure, analogous to survival criteria in evolution.
- **The developer confidence premium is under threat**: The assumption that developers can always re-enter the job market at higher pay after time away may not hold if manual coding skills atrophy rapidly and interviewing norms haven't caught up to value AI-native workflows.
