# They Lied to Us About AI

## Summary

Mo Bitar opens with a sharp critique of OpenAI's organizational dysfunction in early 2026—a company selling AI-powered productivity that couldn't manage its own product sprawl, ultimately resorting to a "code red" meeting and a new executive hire rather than leveraging the tools it sells. He contrasts this with Anthropic's disciplined focus on coding tools and enterprise, which drove explosive adoption (e.g., "Claude benders" over Christmas) despite fewer resources. The irony is pointed: the company with the best AI in the world demonstrated its limits by solving an organizational problem with PowerPoints and meetings.

The video's central empirical claim draws on a randomized controlled study of 16 senior developers using AI on real codebases—finding they were **19% slower** with AI assistance. Despite 41% of code now being AI-generated, velocity isn't increasing. Bitar's explanation: AI-generated code isn't replacing work, it's creating review overhead and technical debt, offloading cognitive labor onto senior engineers rather than eliminating it.

The sharpest argument is reserved for the AGI distinction. Bitar contends the industry raised $300 billion on "AI" hype, then quietly moved the goalposts to "AGI" when LLMs proved not to be general intelligence. LLMs are pattern-matching autocomplete systems that cannot learn on the job or handle the specific, undocumented, proprietary complexity that constitutes most real company work. The thing that could theoretically replace workers doesn't exist; the thing that exists can't replace them.

## Key Concepts

### The Productivity Paradox of AI-Generated Code
A cited RCT study (Metr) found senior developers using AI were 19% slower on real tasks despite 41% of all code being AI-generated. The explanation offered: AI doesn't eliminate work—it shifts it. Generated code must be reviewed, corrected, and integrated by experienced engineers, creating a second job rather than eliminating the first. The "code is going into the trash" framing captures how AI output may increase apparent activity without increasing throughput.

### Focus as Competitive Advantage (Anthropic vs. OpenAI)
Bitar uses the OpenAI/Anthropic contrast as a case study in strategic focus. OpenAI's product sprawl (Sora, Atlas browser, shopping features) dispersed attention and created internal confusion. Anthropic—with less money, fewer people, and less compute—picked two bets (coding tools, enterprise) and outcompeted on both. This is a practical argument that AI-era competition still rewards disciplined prioritization over capability maximalism.

### LLMs vs. AGI: The Moving Goalpost
The video argues the industry performed a bait-and-switch: release LLMs, market them as "AI," collect investment and user growth, then when limitations become obvious, reframe the promise around AGI (which is undefined and undemonstrated). LLMs are probabilistic next-token predictors trained on generic internet data—they cannot learn company-specific context, internal tooling, or undocumented institutional knowledge. The work that matters most to companies is precisely what LLMs are worst at.

### Company-Specific Complexity as the Moat
A recurring theme is that real enterprise work consists of "super specific, super custom stuff that requires deep judgment and company context that doesn't exist anywhere on the internet." LLMs trained on public data are generically capable but specifically limited. The more unique a company's stack, processes, and domain knowledge, the less transferable LLM capability becomes—making proprietary complexity both a challenge and a protection against automation.

### Organizational Dysfunction as AI Critique
The OpenAI code red story functions as more than anecdote—it illustrates that even the best AI tools don't solve coordination, strategy, or focus problems. Organizations still fail at fundamentally human organizational challenges regardless of AI access. This is a subtle but important corrective to the "10x productivity" framing: AI amplifies execution capacity but doesn't substitute for organizational clarity.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Don't measure AI value by code volume.** 41% AI-generated code with slower overall velocity suggests the right metric is reviewed, shipped, working code—not generation rate. Track review overhead and rework, not just output.
- **Treat AI code review as a first-class engineering cost.** If senior engineers are reviewing AI output at 11pm, the productivity gain is being consumed in review. Factor review time into any honest AI ROI calculation.
- **Narrow AI tool deployment to high-repetition, well-documented domains.** LLMs perform best on tasks that appear frequently in public training data. Internal tools, proprietary systems, and undocumented workflows are poor fits—which is most of what distinguishes one company from another.
- **Apply the Anthropic lesson to your own roadmap.** Focused AI adoption in specific high-value areas (e.g., code review, test generation, documentation) outperforms broad unfocused adoption across every workflow simultaneously.
- **Distinguish AI capability claims by timeline.** When executives say AI will replace roles, ask whether they mean current LLMs or future AGI. These are categorically different claims with different evidence bases and different response strategies.
