# 282: Disposable Software: The Trend 90% of People are Getting Wrong--The Hidden Costs We Need to Consider

> **Creator**: AI News & Strategy Daily | Nate B Jones | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-01-20 | **Duration**: 25:21

## Summary

Nate B. Jones argues that "disposable software" is being widely misunderstood because people are pattern-matching to a vibe without analyzing its actual economic and organizational implications. The core claim is simple: the cost of *generating* software has collapsed toward zero, but the cost of *directing attention* toward a goal has not. This inversion is real and significant, but the conclusions most people draw from it—that enterprise SaaS is dead, that planning is obsolete, that every company should ship like Cursor—are wrong because they conflate two entirely different phenomena under one label.

The video distinguishes between two kinds of disposable software: (1) **personal/throwaway software** for one-off use cases that simply didn't exist as a category before AI coding tools, which represents genuine democratization; and (2) **disposable features within enterprise products**, where the "ship everything constantly, let users sort it out" philosophy is only coherent for a narrow slice of the market. Cursor can operate this way because its customers are developers—the most change-tolerant population of software users on Earth. Enterprise customers buying Salesforce, Workday, or payroll systems are explicitly paying *not* to think about software. They're buying reliability and peace of mind, not velocity.

A key underappreciated cost is the maintenance burden that persists even when initial build costs collapse. AI-generated code accumulates technical debt, breaks when APIs change, and introduces security vulnerabilities (reportedly in nearly half of all coding tasks)—and someone, usually a highly paid builder, still has to remediate all of that. The "vibe code your own Salesforce to save $100/seat" argument fails because it ignores the opportunity cost of diverting skilled builders from their core business mission to maintain internal tooling that is not their core competency.

## Key Concepts

### Attention as the Durable Constraint
The central thesis of the video is that software cost was never the binding constraint—attention was. When code generation becomes essentially free, people assume the constraint is lifted, but specifying goals, configuring agents, reviewing outputs, debugging failures, and maintaining running systems all still consume human attention. Cheap code does not produce more hours in the day for your best engineers. The opportunity cost of redirecting skilled builders away from core product work to maintain internally vibe-coded tooling remains very high regardless of how cheap the initial generation was.

### Two Distinct Phenomena Under One Label
"Disposable software" describes two economically and strategically different realities. The first is personal/throwaway software—dashboards, one-off apps, family vacation tools—a genuinely new category that democratizes software access (75% of Replit's users reportedly never write a line of code). The second is disposable features inside enterprise products, where teams ship constantly and let customer response determine what gets hardened. These require completely different strategic responses and confusing them leads to bad decisions.

### Customer Tolerance as the Strategic Boundary
The viability of a "ship everything constantly" philosophy is almost entirely determined by who your customers are. Cursor's customers are developers who understand regressions, can fix broken things, and consciously choose bleeding-edge tools. Enterprise CIOs buy software specifically so they *don't* have to think about it—stability is the product they're purchasing. This isn't a gap that AI will close over time; it's a fundamental difference in what the customer values and is contractually paying for.

### The Hidden Maintenance Burden of AI-Generated Code
The video challenges the assumption that cheap generation equals cheap total cost of ownership. AI-generated code still accumulates technical debt, breaks when underlying dependencies change, and reportedly introduces security vulnerabilities in close to half of coding tasks—often deep architectural issues that automated scanners miss. The cost doesn't disappear; it shifts from upfront engineering to ongoing maintenance and security remediation, still paid by the same expensive people the strategy was supposed to free up.

### The Collapse of Planning Rituals
When software was capital-intensive, all the overhead around it—product specs, roadmaps, stakeholder alignment, user research—existed as insurance against building the wrong expensive thing. When software becomes disposable, those rituals become overhead rather than insurance. Cursor has operationalized this as "code is reality": anything that isn't shipping is waste. This is coherent for their context but is being incorrectly generalized. The vision layer (where you're going and why) remains essential; the planning layer (detailed specification of what to build before you build it) is what becomes optional when iteration is cheap.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Before adopting Cursor-style "ship everything" philosophy, audit your customer base.** If your customers are developers or highly technical early adopters, fast iteration with instability may be acceptable. If they're business buyers with SLAs and compliance requirements, disposable features will destroy trust faster than they create value.
- **Evaluate vibe-coding internal tools against opportunity cost, not just licensing cost.** The question isn't whether $100/seat is cheap to replace with AI-generated code; it's whether the hours your best builders spend specifying, prompting, debugging, and maintaining that internal tool are worth more than those same hours spent on your core product.
- **Treat AI-generated code security as a first-class concern, not an afterthought.** With vulnerabilities reportedly appearing in ~50% of AI coding tasks—particularly deep architectural ones—security review and remediation need to be budgeted as ongoing costs, not assumed away.
- **Separate the vision layer from the planning layer when rethinking process.** You can and should eliminate heavy planning rituals if you're in a fast-iteration context, but you cannot eliminate clear product vision and direction. The "code is reality" philosophy requires knowing *what reality you're building toward* even if you skip the spec documents.
- **Personal/throwaway software and enterprise disposable features require completely different organizational responses.** Don't let success in one category mislead you into applying the same philosophy to the other.
