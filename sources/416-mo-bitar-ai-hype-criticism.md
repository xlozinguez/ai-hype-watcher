# AI Psychosis Is Spreading

## Summary

Mo Bitar delivers a sardonic, line-by-line critique of a viral article ("Something Big Is Happening" by Matt Schumer) that frames current AI capabilities in apocalyptic terms to generate fear, engagement, and ultimately product adoption. The article, which garnered 33 million views, uses classic narrative hooks — vivid imagery, invented milestone dates, and selectively dramatic quotes from AI CEOs — to convince non-technical readers that AI has crossed a threshold into near-sentient autonomy. Bitar systematically dissects each claim, pointing out where the author conflates normal software behavior (a cursor agent opening a browser tab, test-passing iteration loops) with emergent consciousness or self-directed agency.

Bitar's core objection is not that AI tools are unimpressive, but that the discourse around them has become deliberately deceptive. He identifies three overlapping incentives driving the hysteria: media engagement (dramatic framing drives shares), AI company marketing (Anthropic's Dario Amodei calling his models "scary" makes developers want them), and a secondary industry of AI-adjacent founders/influencers who profit from panic-driven tool adoption. The article's conclusion — "start using AI seriously" meaning "outsource all your work to AI" — is presented as helpful advice but functions as a funnel for paid subscriptions.

The broader argument is that AI is a genuinely useful but fundamentally instruction-dependent tool. Computers, including LLMs, require human direction; the gap between what AI can do and what it is being claimed to do is being weaponized. The "real singularity," Bitar quips, is humans losing their minds into believing AI has agency, consciousness, and self-improvement loops rather than engaging critically with what the technology actually does.

## Key Concepts

### AI Hype as Narrative Engineering
Bitar demonstrates how the Schumer article constructs its impact through storytelling techniques rather than technical accuracy: opening with a COVID-19 analogy to trigger visceral memory, fabricating significance around an arbitrary date ("February 5th"), and using present-tense dramatic framing ("the app opens itself") that implies agency where none exists. This is a template for a genre of AI content that prioritizes emotional resonance over accuracy.

### Conflating Tool Behavior With Sentience
Several specific technical misrepresentations are identified: Cursor's built-in browser executing an `open` command becomes "the app opens itself"; test-driven iteration loops become the model "iterating until it's satisfied"; early-version model-assisted debugging becomes "AI building itself." Each substitutes a mundane, deterministic software behavior for a loaded anthropomorphic description. Bitar's corrective is simple: LLMs have no concept of satisfaction, aesthetic judgment, or self-directed goals — they respond to instructions.

### CEO Statements as Marketing, Not Truth-Telling
Bitar reframes quotes from Dario Amodei (Anthropic CEO) as competitive marketing rather than honest assessment. Claiming models are "scary," predicting 50% elimination of entry-level white-collar jobs, and stating "AI is writing much of our code" all function to increase Anthropic's perceived model capability and influence AI safety regulation in ways that disadvantage open-source competitors. Treating these statements as neutral, credible data points — as Schumer's article does — is a category error.

### The "Outsourcing" Framing Problem
The article's action advice boils down to "outsource your work to AI," which Bitar flags as both intellectually dishonest and practically counterproductive. Genuine AI-assisted development requires deep technical understanding to direct, validate, and correct model output — the opposite of disengaged outsourcing. Framing AI use as "outsourcing" obscures the skill required to use it well and discourages the learning that makes AI genuinely powerful.

### The Instruction Dependency of Computers
Bitar's foundational counter-argument is architectural: computers, including the most capable LLMs, are instruction-following systems. Some entity with agency must direct them. This doesn't diminish their utility, but it permanently forecloses the "AI replacing human judgment entirely" narrative. The implication for workers is not panic but continued relevance — someone has to give the instructions, define quality, and validate the output.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Read AI capability claims skeptically by checking what the underlying behavior actually is.** When an article says "the AI does X," ask what the literal software action is — often it's a mundane, deterministic step being described in anthropomorphic terms.
- **Treat AI CEO public statements as marketing communications, not technical briefings.** Incentives (valuation, regulation, competitive positioning) heavily shape what founders say publicly about their models' capabilities or dangers.
- **"Using AI seriously" requires more engagement, not less.** Effective AI-assisted development demands technical judgment to frame tasks, evaluate outputs, and iterate — the opposite of passive outsourcing.
- **Viral AI content often follows a formula:** emotional hook → milestone date → dramatic capability claim → CEO quote → call to action (buy the paid tier). Recognizing the template helps evaluate the content.
- **The human-as-director role is durable.** As long as LLMs are instruction-following systems, someone with domain knowledge and judgment must provide direction — that role does not disappear with capability improvements.
