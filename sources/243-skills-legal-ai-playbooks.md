# 243: SKILLS Legal AI Use Case Demos: Playbook Generation

> **Creator**: SKILLS | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-06 | **Duration**: 50:49

## Summary

A community seminar hosted by the SKILLS legal technology community, moderated by Wendy Chu from Disney, showcasing four legal AI platforms competing in the playbook generation space: Chameleio, Filevine (Lois/Pinsites), Legora, and Ruly AI. Each vendor demonstrates how their platform automates the creation, management, and enforcement of legal negotiation playbooks -- rule sets that codify an organization's preferred contract positions, fallback language, and risk thresholds.

The session concludes with market analysis from Nikki at Legal TechHub, who shares survey data from 130 law firms showing Harvey leading adoption at 26.2% with a long tail of competitors. She frames a key market shift: playbook technology was historically reserved for corporate legal departments with standardized contracts, but AI-powered real-time playbook generation now makes the technology viable for law firms handling diverse, client-specific deal flows.

## Key Concepts

### AI-Powered Playbook Generation From Precedent

All four platforms demonstrate the ability to auto-generate playbook rules by analyzing existing contracts, templates, and negotiation history. Rather than manually writing rules clause by clause, legal teams upload marked-up documents and the AI extracts patterns -- preferred positions, fallback language, and unacceptable terms. Filevine/Pinsites particularly emphasizes learning from actual negotiation behavior: what you accept, change, and reject across deals gets distilled into playbook rules over time.

### Living Playbooks With Continuous Learning

A recurring theme across vendors is that playbooks should not be static documents. Chameleio demonstrates a recommendation engine that observes how lawyers deviate from playbook rules during real negotiations and surfaces suggestions for playbook updates, weighted by the type of deviation (dismissal vs. active edit vs. explicit feedback). Critically, all vendors emphasize human-in-the-loop approval -- playbook changes require editor-level permissions and explicit acceptance.

### Word Add-in as the Enforcement Layer

Every platform centers its enforcement workflow on Microsoft Word add-ins that apply playbook rules directly during contract review. The pattern is consistent: upload or open a document, select a playbook, run the review, receive clause-by-clause recommendations with risk ratings and source citations, then apply changes with tracked revisions and auto-generated comments for opposing counsel. This positions AI as an active participant in the negotiation workflow rather than a separate research tool.

### Market Fragmentation and the Law Firm vs. Corporate Legal Divide

The market analysis reveals significant fragmentation. Harvey leads with ~26% live adoption among law firms, but the long tail of single-digit competitors indicates the market is still in flux. Nikki highlights a structural difference: corporate legal departments have long used playbooks (standardized contracts, defined positions), while law firms historically found them less useful due to deal diversity. AI-powered real-time playbook generation that can be customized per client is what makes this technology newly relevant for law firms.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Playbook generation from precedent is now table stakes**: All four platforms can auto-generate rules from uploaded contracts, but the differentiator is how well they learn from ongoing negotiation behavior
- **Principles-based rules beat clause-mapped rules**: Filevine/Pinsites argues that writing playbook rules as general principles (rather than mapping to specific template clauses) makes playbooks resilient to template changes
- **The enforcement point is Microsoft Word**: Every vendor anchors enforcement in the Word add-in workflow, suggesting that legal AI adoption hinges on meeting lawyers where they already work
- **Playbook admin permissions matter**: Multiple vendors emphasize role-based access control for playbook modifications, preventing accidental updates from junior reviewers
- **The market is early and fragmented**: With Harvey at 26% and a long tail of competitors, legal AI playbook technology is still being evaluated -- no single vendor has locked up the market
