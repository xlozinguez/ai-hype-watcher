# I Don't Trust Anthropic

## Summary

Mo Bitar argues that Anthropic's public-facing identity as a safety-first, mission-driven AI research company is largely a marketing veneer designed to recruit talent and differentiate itself from OpenAI — not a genuine philosophical commitment. The video was prompted by Anthropic releasing ads mocking OpenAI's planned ad-supported tier, which Sam Altman publicly called out as "doublespeak." Bitar uses this moment as a jumping-off point to catalog a pattern of behavior he sees as hypocritical: claiming not to race on capabilities while actively leading the frontier, advocating for regulation publicly while lobbying for permissive rules privately, and banning competitors from their platform while calling themselves a public benefit corporation.

A central thread is Bitar's reading of an Effective Altruism forum post arguing that Anthropic's original stated mission — to study AI safety by *following* the frontier rather than pushing it — was quietly abandoned and may have been a recruiting tactic from the start. He contrasts this with OpenAI, which he describes approvingly as transparently commercial ("what you see is what you get"), and with Yann LeCun's open-source advocacy at Meta, which Bitar frames as the more genuinely safety-conscious approach: keeping powerful AI out of the control of any single gatekeeper.

The video's broader point is a critique of mission-washing in AI: companies that layer philosophical or altruistic narratives over fundamentally competitive, capital-driven ambitions. Bitar sees this as particularly dangerous in the AI space because the stakes of who controls model access are enormous — being deplatformed from a dominant AI provider in a world where AI is essential infrastructure is, in his framing, existentially serious.

## Key Concepts

### Mission-Washing as Competitive Strategy
Bitar argues that Anthropic's safety-and-ethics identity is primarily a *differentiation tactic* rather than a core operating principle. Because OpenAI has a dominant brand and head start, the only viable positioning for a challenger is to claim moral superiority. The ads attacking OpenAI's ad plans are read as an extension of this strategy — not a principled stand, but a competitive move dressed up in altruistic language.

### The Doublespeak Pattern
Bitar highlights a recurring gap between Anthropic's public statements and internal behavior: (1) claiming not to push AI capabilities while arguably leading the coding/enterprise frontier; (2) testifying in favor of strict AI regulation while reportedly lobbying for permissive rules in private; (3) positioning as a public benefit corporation while banning competitors, including OpenAI and xAI engineers, from using their services under broad terms-of-service clauses. Sam Altman's use of "doublespeak" crystallizes this critique.

### Open Source as the Genuine Safety Path
Bitar endorses Yann LeCun's framing: the real safety risk is centralization of AI in the hands of a few gatekeepers, not the open availability of models. He argues that the "AI is dangerous, open source is reckless" narrative is itself propaganda that benefits incumbent closed-model providers — Anthropic chief among them — by suppressing competition and justifying access control. Open source, in this view, is what prevents the scenario where being banned from a dominant AI platform becomes an existential threat.

### Talent Recruitment as the Real Audience for Safety Messaging
A commenter's observation that Bitar quotes approvingly: the safety narrative and the anti-OpenAI ads are not aimed at consumers but at a narrow pool of elite researchers who have made enough money that purpose and mission matter more than salary. Anthropic's positioning as "the good guys" is a recruiting instrument targeting people who would otherwise join or stay at OpenAI.

### The Public Benefit Corporation Gap
Anthropic is structured as a public benefit corporation, which carries implied obligations to broader societal good. Bitar argues this framing is undermined by practices like prohibiting use of Claude to build competing AI products — a standard anti-competitive clause that sits awkwardly alongside claims of democratizing AI access and benefiting the public.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Evaluate AI company claims against revealed behavior, not stated values.** Terms of service, lobbying positions, and product decisions are more reliable signals of actual priorities than press releases or mission statements.
- **Dependency on any single closed-model provider is a risk.** As AI becomes critical infrastructure, the ability to be deplatformed without recourse is a real operational and strategic exposure — worth factoring into tool choices for teams and organizations.
- **Open-source models reduce single-point-of-failure risk.** For builders who want to avoid being subject to arbitrary access decisions, open-weight models (LLaMA, etc.) provide continuity guarantees that API-only providers cannot.
- **"Safety" rhetoric in AI can function as regulatory capture.** When dominant players advocate loudest for regulation, consider who benefits from higher compliance costs and barriers to entry — often the incumbents proposing the rules.
- **Mission-driven framing at AI companies may primarily serve HR and PR.** Internal incentives at any well-funded startup remain financial; teams should calibrate expectations accordingly when evaluating culture claims from AI vendors.
