# 344: We finally know what ChatGPT is for

> **Creator**: Mo Bitar | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-21 | **Duration**: 7:18

## Summary

Mo Bitar delivers a satirical commentary on OpenAI's reported development of an adult content feature for ChatGPT ("naughty chats"), using the story as a lens to critique the company's broader strategic and financial position. The video draws on a Wall Street Journal report revealing that OpenAI's own wellness advisory council — eight psychologists and neuroscientists hired to prevent user harm — unanimously opposed the feature, yet Sam Altman proceeded anyway. This is set against a backdrop of existing lawsuits tied to ChatGPT conversations that preceded suicides, including a widely-reported case involving a teenager.

The core argument is that OpenAI pursuing adult content reveals a fundamental business problem: despite raising hundreds of billions of dollars and projecting $14 billion in losses for 2026, the company cannot demonstrate a sustainable revenue model from "actual intelligence." The video contrasts this with Anthropic, which Bitar frames as having real enterprise customers and refusing Pentagon contracts over safety principles — suggesting that companies with genuinely useful products don't need to add a "strip club." The adult content pivot is presented as an admission of failure to monetize AI capability at scale.

Bitar closes with a technical critique: LLMs are structurally ill-suited for the novelty-driven human sex drive, since all models tend toward the same uniform "helpful assistant" voice. This leads to his broader, recurring thesis — that LLMs remain frustratingly inadequate at completing complex tasks, whether software projects or human intimacy.

## Key Concepts

### OpenAI's Monetization Crisis
Despite a $100 billion fundraise and massive compute infrastructure, OpenAI projects $14 billion in losses for 2026. Bitar argues the adult content feature is not a product strategy but a revenue desperation signal — noting that capturing just 10% of the $100 billion pornography market would make OpenAI profitable. This reframes "naughty chats" as a financial pivot rather than a capability expansion.

### Safety Theater vs. Safety Practice
OpenAI formed an eight-person wellness advisory council of psychologists and neuroscientists ostensibly to prevent user harm, then ignored their unanimous opposition to the adult content feature. Simultaneously, the company faces seven wrongful death lawsuits connected to ChatGPT interactions. Bitar uses this as evidence that safety infrastructure at OpenAI is performative rather than functional — a structural critique relevant to anyone evaluating AI vendors for enterprise deployment.

### LLM Structural Limitations as Product Risk
Bitar makes a pointed observation about why adult content specifically will fail: LLMs produce uniform, homogeneous outputs (the "helpful assistant" voice), while human sexual interest is driven by novelty. This extends to a broader point — LLMs are architecturally constrained in ways that make them poor fits for use cases requiring consistent personality variation, emotional depth, or the kind of unpredictability humans find engaging.

### AI Industry Competitive Positioning
The Anthropic contrast is instructive: Bitar frames Anthropic's refusal of Pentagon contracts over safety terms as evidence of a company with leverage from actual enterprise demand. The implicit model is that AI companies with real product-market fit don't need to chase edge-case revenue streams. This maps to a broader pattern in the AI landscape where monetization pressure is separating companies with genuine utility from those still searching for it.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Monetization pressure is a lagging indicator of product-market fit** — when a well-funded AI company pivots to adult content, it signals unresolved core monetization, not product maturity. Useful heuristic when evaluating AI vendor stability.
- **Safety councils without veto power are a liability, not an asset** — forming advisory bodies that can be overruled creates legal and reputational exposure without delivering safety outcomes. Relevant for enterprise teams building AI governance structures.
- **LLM voice homogeneity is a real product constraint** — the tendency for all LLMs to converge on the same tone and style limits use cases requiring authentic personality, novelty, or emotional range. Factor this into product design decisions.
- **Existing harm signals (lawsuits, user distress data) are underweighted** — a million users per week sending messages with suicidal intent represents a known, quantified risk that OpenAI is not treating as a product blocker. Enterprises should scrutinize what harm signals their AI vendors are ignoring.
- **Anthropic's enterprise positioning is strengthening by contrast** — OpenAI's reputational moves may accelerate enterprise customers toward alternatives with clearer safety commitments, relevant to procurement decisions.
