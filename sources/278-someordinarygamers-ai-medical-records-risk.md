# 278: Microslop Wants Your Medical Records Now...

> **Creator**: SomeOrdinaryGamers | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-13 | **Duration**: 18:29

## Summary

SomeOrdinaryGamers (Mudahar) covers Microsoft's "Copilot Health" feature, which aims to aggregate users' medical records, wearable data, and health history into a single AI-powered dashboard. The video is primarily a critical commentary arguing that giving private medical information to large tech companies like Microsoft is a serious privacy and safety risk, regardless of the security assurances these companies provide. Mudahar frames this as part of a broader pattern of AI companies chasing new monetization vectors by overstating the reliability and trustworthiness of LLM-based systems.

The core technical critique is that LLMs are probability engines — pattern-matching text predictors — not sentient reasoning beings, and that deploying them in high-stakes domains like medicine creates dangerous conditions when users treat AI output as authoritative. Mudahar illustrates this with a personal near-death experience from a perforated appendix, noting that his diagnosis required two days of hands-on clinical testing, blood draws, ultrasound, and CT scanning — a process no LLM could replicate or replace. He also cites a real-world example of a patient who suffered for a year because they trusted a ChatGPT hallucination about drug side effects over their doctor.

The video broadens its argument to include analogous risks in AI-assisted coding (vibe coding producing insecure software without human review) and AI-assisted military targeting (the Palantir/drone strike example where bad data and AI output allegedly caused civilian casualties). The through-line is that AI hype is conditioning ordinary people to accept AI judgment in domains where hallucination is not merely an inconvenience but a life-threatening failure mode.

## Key Concepts

### LLMs as Probability Engines, Not Reasoners
Mudahar repeatedly emphasizes that LLMs are not sentient, are not reasoning, and are not capable of the kind of contextual, multi-modal, embodied clinical judgment that medicine requires. They produce statistically plausible text, which can look authoritative while being dangerously wrong. This distinction is critical for any domain where being wrong has irreversible consequences.

### Privacy Risk of Medical Data with Tech Platforms
The video argues that no amount of corporate assurance — encryption in transit, "dedicated secure spaces," meaningful control — is verifiable by the end user. Tech companies can change terms of service unilaterally, and their core business interests are misaligned with genuine data privacy. Feeding medical records to Microsoft or OpenAI is framed as an irreversible privacy exposure regardless of current policy language.

### Hallucination in High-Stakes Domains
The patient anecdote (ChatGPT confusing intranasal steroids with systemic glucocorticoids) illustrates a concrete failure mode: users don't know enough about a domain to frame questions correctly, can't evaluate whether the answer is accurate, and may not even know when to seek a second opinion. This compounds AI hallucination risk in medicine, law, and finance relative to lower-stakes domains.

### AI Hype as Product Marketing
Copilot Health's language — "medical super intelligence," "diagnostic orchestrator," "coherent story of your health" — is analyzed as marketing designed to make LLM products feel more valuable and trustworthy than their actual capabilities warrant. Mudahar connects this to the broader AGI/ASI hype cycle, noting that models still hallucinate at high rates even as companies claim proximity to superhuman intelligence.

### Vibe Coding Security Risk (Analogy)
Briefly but pointedly, Mudahar draws a parallel between AI-generated medical advice and AI-generated code being shipped without human review. In both cases, the absence of a domain-expert human in the loop creates compounding risk — insecure code gets deployed, and medically incorrect AI output gets acted upon — because the end user lacks the expertise to evaluate the output.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Do not upload personal medical records to commercial AI platforms**, regardless of stated privacy policies; terms of service can change, and you cannot independently verify data handling.
- **Treat AI health tools as search engine supplements, not diagnostic authorities** — they may help you form questions for a real doctor, but the AI cannot replicate clinical examination, lab work, or the interpretive judgment of an experienced physician.
- **Recognize the pattern-matching limitation**: an LLM answering a medical question is predicting plausible text about medicine, not reasoning through your specific clinical situation — the output can be confidently wrong in ways neither you nor the AI can detect.
- **Apply the same skepticism to high-stakes AI outputs across domains**: AI-generated code, AI-assisted legal filings, AI-driven targeting systems — all share the same hallucination risk, and the consequences scale with how irreversible the domain's errors are.
- **Monitor terms of service changes on AI platforms** where you have shared sensitive data; the privacy guarantees offered at signup are not permanent contractual obligations.
