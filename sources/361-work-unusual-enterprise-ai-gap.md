# 361: AI Isn’t Delivering (And CEOs Know It)

> **Creator**: Work. Unusual | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-22 | **Duration**: 13:25

## Summary

Drawing on data from PWC, McKinsey, Deloitte, and Gartner, this video argues that despite CEO hype, AI is not yet delivering measurable business results at scale. Fewer than 12% of CEOs report measurable revenue growth or cost reduction from AI, and less than 2% of companies have fully scaled a generative AI solution within even a single business function. The gap between executive enthusiasm and operational reality is wide and largely unacknowledged in public discourse.

The core diagnosis is that most enterprises are running scattered, localized AI pilots — employees using LLMs to write newsletters or automate a finance process — without any consistent measurement framework or path to scale. AI is being treated like a spreadsheet tool (Excel) rather than a platform-level transformation (cloud), meaning wins stay siloed and can't compound across the organization. No incumbent company has yet fully restructured itself around AI.

The video identifies four structural problems: AI is a general-purpose tool rather than a solution mapped to a defined problem; most employees lack the process-engineering skills to translate local experiments into scale gains; bespoke enterprise-grade AI solutions don't yet exist for most industries; and the cost-reduction framing dominates over revenue generation, which limits AI's transformative potential. The net picture is that AI is "everywhere and invisible" — present as a strategic mandate but absent as a measurable result.

## Key Concepts

### The Excel vs. Cloud Distinction
Most companies are deploying AI the way they deploy spreadsheets: as flexible, localized tools that individuals customize for their own workflows. This produces real but ungeneralizable wins. Platform-scale transformation (analogous to cloud migration) requires enterprise-wide architecture, consistent measurement, and deliberate change management — none of which most organizations have applied to AI yet.

### Strategy Without Tactics ("Haben wir auch so ein Ding")
Executive AI strategy is being driven by competitive mimicry and abstract belief rather than defined business cases. The German phrase invoked — roughly "we need one of those things too" — captures the dynamic: companies mandate AI adoption without clear answers to *which problem*, *which function*, *which metric*. This produces scattered operational effort and makes ROI nearly impossible to attribute.

### The Measurement Gap
Even where AI is producing genuine efficiency gains, companies lack frameworks to quantify them consistently. Customer service response times improve, but connecting that to revenue is difficult. An employee saving two hours a week on a financial report creates real value, but it can't be aggregated, audited, or scaled without deliberate instrumentation. The absence of measurement infrastructure is as much a blocker as the absence of AI capability.

### AI as Tool vs. AI as Solution
Enterprise software purchases (Salesforce, CNC machines) solve defined problems. AI in its current form is more like a hammer: a general-purpose instrument that *could* address many problems but requires the buyer to engineer the solution themselves. Until bespoke, commercially proven AI solutions exist for specific industries and functions — legal, R&D, manufacturing — most companies will struggle to move past the pilot stage.

### Cost Focus vs. Value Creation
CEO ambitions are predominantly cost-reduction oriented (cutting headcount, automating support). But cost savings eventually equalize as electricity and infrastructure costs rise. Durable competitive advantage requires AI to be *additive* — generating new revenue, enabling new products, unlocking new markets. The current enterprise AI landscape is weak on this dimension, and even the model-builders haven't yet converted infrastructure spend into durable profit.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Don't benchmark your organization against AI-native companies (Amazon, Google, Meta).** Their cost structures and revenue models are categorically different; their AI wins don't transfer to traditional incumbents.
- **Treat the absence of a measurement framework as a first-order problem.** Before scaling any AI pilot, define the metric it moves and the baseline it's measured against — otherwise wins stay invisible and die in the next budget cycle.
- **Expect your workforce to use AI like Excel, not cloud.** Localized productivity gains are the realistic near-term outcome; don't confuse employee enthusiasm for LLMs with organizational transformation capability.
- **Be skeptical of enterprise AI solutions in your sector.** For most industries, bespoke, thoroughly tested, commercially proven AI solutions don't yet exist — which means any serious deployment requires internal engineering investment, not just vendor procurement.
- **Revenue generation is the harder and more important problem.** If your AI strategy is purely cost-focused, it has a built-in ceiling. The long-term case requires identifying where AI can create new value, not just reduce existing costs.
