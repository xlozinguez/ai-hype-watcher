# 325: They lied to us about AI

> **Creator**: Mo Bitar | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-03-19 | **Duration**: 7:16

## Summary

Mo Bitar opens with a sharp critique of OpenAI's organizational chaos in early 2026 — sprawling projects, a "code red" meeting called by a newly hired Facebook executive, and a string of unfocused product launches (Sora, a browser, shopping features) that left the company less productive than its own productivity pitch promised. The contrast with Anthropic is pointed: far fewer resources, but disciplined focus on coding tools and enterprise, yielding Claude Code's explosive adoption. The punchline is that the company selling AI-driven productivity couldn't manage its own.

The more substantive argument is about a randomized controlled study by "Metr" (likely METR) involving 16 senior developers on real codebases. Contrary to the prevailing narrative, developers using AI were **19% slower** — not faster. Bitar's explanation: AI generates code that still requires expert review and cleanup, creating a hidden second job for senior engineers rather than eliminating work. The 41% of code now written by AI isn't accelerating output; it's flooding review queues.

The video closes with a pointed structural critique of the AI industry's language: LLMs were called "AI," then when limitations became obvious, the goalposts shifted to "AGI" — a technology that doesn't yet exist and may not be theoretically possible. The real tool (LLMs) can't replace knowledge workers because it can't learn company-specific context on the job; the imaginary tool (AGI) is being used to manufacture existential fear and justify hundreds of billions in investment. Bitar argues the industry is counting on most people not noticing this sleight of hand.

## Key Concepts

### The Productivity Paradox of AI Coding Tools
A controlled study of 16 senior developers on real codebases found AI-assisted development made them 19% *slower*, not faster. Even as 41% of code is now AI-generated, output velocity hasn't meaningfully increased. The explanation: AI shifts work rather than eliminating it — generating plausible-looking code that still requires expert review, debugging, and rejection. Senior developers become gatekeepers of AI output, adding a second job on top of their existing one.

### Focus as the Real Competitive Moat
Anthropic's competitive advantage over OpenAI wasn't model quality, compute, or headcount — it was product focus. By concentrating entirely on coding tools and enterprise, and skipping browsers, video feeds, and image generation, Anthropic captured the highest-value developer workflow market. The lesson for AI-assisted teams mirrors the lesson for AI labs: diffuse effort across too many surfaces and you underperform a smaller, focused competitor.

### LLM vs. AGI: A Definitional Bait-and-Switch
The video makes an explicit structural argument about industry framing. LLMs were marketed as "AI," then when their limitations (no reasoning, no on-the-job learning, pattern-matching over comprehension) became apparent, the industry rebranded the eventual destination as "AGI." This allows companies to simultaneously defend current products ("look how much it does") and maintain existential urgency ("the real thing is coming"). Bitar frames this as a deliberate misdirection to sustain $300B+ in investment.

### Company-Specific Context as the Irreplaceable Core
LLMs are trained on generic internet data. Real enterprise work lives in proprietary systems, undocumented internal tools, and institutional judgment that has never been written down. This is precisely the work that defines a company's operations — and precisely what an LLM cannot access or learn. The more idiosyncratic and contextual the work, the less an LLM can substitute for human expertise. This structural gap isn't a bug to be fixed; it's inherent to how LLMs function.

### Organizational Dysfunction Doesn't Disappear with Better AI
OpenAI's code red — too many competing projects, unclear priorities, coordination failures — was solved with a PowerPoint presentation and management restructuring, not an AI agent. This irony underscores that AI tools address a narrow class of knowledge work problems. Strategic alignment, prioritization, and organizational coherence remain fundamentally human coordination problems. The most AI-forward company in the world still needed a manager from Facebook.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Don't measure AI coding success by lines or percentage of AI-written code** — measure end-to-end cycle time including review, rework, and integration. The METR study suggests raw generation volume is a vanity metric that can mask slowdown.
- **Senior developer time is the actual bottleneck** when AI code generation scales — invest in review tooling, clear AI code conventions, and explicit PR policies before expanding AI generation footprint.
- **Treat AGI timelines as irrelevant to current planning** — build workflows around what LLMs demonstrably do (pattern completion on well-documented tasks) not around speculative general reasoning capabilities.
- **Scope AI tool adoption as narrowly as Anthropic scoped its product** — one high-value workflow done well (e.g., code review, test generation, documentation) outperforms broad deployment across many surface areas.
- **Document company-specific context explicitly** — since LLMs can only operate on what's been written down, the ROI on internal documentation, runbooks, and decision logs increases substantially in an AI-assisted environment.
