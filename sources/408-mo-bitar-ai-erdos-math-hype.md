# 408: The truth behind AI “solving” the Erdos problems

> **Creator**: Mo Bitar | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-02-10 | **Duration**: 10:19

## Summary

Mo Bitar responds to claims that AI is "solving" long-standing Erdős mathematical problems by examining what's actually happening beneath the headlines. Drawing on a New Scientist article, he argues that LLMs are functioning primarily as extraordinarily capable search engines — surfacing relevant papers and forgotten solutions from the mathematical literature — rather than generating genuinely novel proofs through reasoning. Notably, five of six "AI-solved" Erdős problems turned out to have already been solved in existing literature that simply hadn't been found.

The core argument is that LLMs are not intelligent in any meaningful sense — they are statistical pattern matchers that process vast corpora and surface non-trivial connections between documents. The problems being addressed are also characterized as relatively straightforward by professional mathematicians, and many had received little attention simply because researchers prioritize higher-ROI problems. This mirrors software development, where backlogs fill with low-priority but solvable tasks that no one has time to address.

Bitar positions this as net-positive news for human intellectual workers rather than a displacement threat. LLMs amplify human capacity by handling the exhaustive search and synthesis work that humans lack the time or breadth to do — enabling researchers and developers to tackle problems they previously couldn't justify spending time on. The result, he argues, is not a replacement of human intellectual work but a renaissance: more problems get solved, more connections get made, and humans are freed to focus on genuinely hard, high-value problems.

## Key Concepts

### LLMs as Advanced Search Engines, Not Reasoners
The primary mechanism behind AI's mathematical "breakthroughs" is retrieval and synthesis, not original reasoning. LLMs scoured the mathematical literature and found that several Erdős problems had already been solved in obscure or forgotten papers. The value is in the breadth of information processed and the non-trivial statistical relationships surfaced across that corpus — not in the generation of novel proofs.

### The Attention Bottleneck in Knowledge Work
Many unsolved problems — in mathematics and software alike — are unsolved not because they're intractable, but because skilled people prioritize higher-ROI work. LLMs remove this bottleneck by applying tireless, unlimited resources to low-attention problems. This is the same dynamic developers experience when AI tools help them "obliterate the backlog."

### AI Hype vs. Mechanistic Reality
Claims about AI "solving" complex problems often obscure the actual mechanism. The Erdős case follows a pattern similar to other inflated AI claims (the "Claudebot incident" reference): surface-level results look impressive until examined carefully. Understanding *how* AI produces a result matters as much as whether it produces it.

### Information Indexing as a Core AI Value Proposition
Humanity has accumulated far more knowledge than any indexing system (including Google) can effectively surface. LLMs represent a qualitative leap in information retrieval at scale — analogous to how Gregor Mendel's foundational genetics work was lost for 16 years before being rediscovered. AI makes such losses far less likely going forward.

### Human Amplifier vs. Human Replacer
The distinction Bitar draws is between tools that replace human judgment and tools that extend human capacity. LLMs handle the breadth problem (processing petabytes of information) while humans handle the depth problem (creative problem-solving, forming emergent connections, evaluating significance). Developers working with AI tools are burning out from working *more*, not less — a sign of amplification, not displacement.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Verify the mechanism behind AI capability claims** — when AI reportedly "solves" a hard problem, check whether it's generating new ideas or retrieving and synthesizing existing ones. The distinction has major implications for how you use the tool.
- **Use LLMs for literature and reference search** as a primary workflow, especially in domains with large, fragmented corpora (research, legacy codebases, documentation). This is where they demonstrably outperform traditional search.
- **Treat the backlog as now tractable** — problems shelved for lack of time or interest are newly accessible. In software, this means low-priority bug fixes, refactors, and minor features that AI can handle without demanding senior attention.
- **Resist hype in both directions** — neither dismiss AI capabilities nor overclaim intelligence or understanding. The honest frame is a powerful, tireless, broad-but-shallow pattern-matching tool that complements narrow-but-deep human expertise.
- **Expect productivity gains to increase workload, not reduce it** — empirically, developers with AI tools are working more, not less, as the ceiling on what they can accomplish rises. Plan accordingly rather than assuming headcount can be reduced.
