# CS Grads Are Giving Up

## Summary

Coding Jesus (CJ) reacts to a video from a 2026 CS graduate named Timmy McAllister who, despite a 4.0 GPA from a top school, cannot land a paid software engineering job. CJ uses the video as a springboard to diagnose two intertwined problems: a structurally weak resume that fails to differentiate the candidate from a vibe coder, and a broader job market reality where junior/entry-level positions are being compressed by AI tooling and a flood of applicants. The core argument is that the traditional "head down, get the GPA, apply online" strategy is no longer sufficient — networking, hustle, and strong self-presentation have become mandatory, not optional.

The video also briefly engages with Timmy's more systemic critique — that AI is eliminating entry-level work across white-collar fields — which CJ partially agrees with while pushing back on the "system is to blame" framing. CJ's position is that individual agency (networking, resume quality, negotiating even unpaid internships into stipends) is still the operative lever available to job seekers, even if structural headwinds are real. Senior and principal engineering roles remain in demand; it is specifically the junior layer that is under acute pressure.

The vibe coding phenomenon is implicitly central throughout: CJ's main resume critique is that Timmy's bullet points are indistinguishable from someone who just prompted an AI to build things, with no technical depth or specificity. In a market saturated with AI-assisted projects, the inability to signal genuine under-the-hood competence is a decisive disadvantage.

## Key Concepts

### Resume Depth as Signal Against Vibe Coding
CJ argues that vague resume bullets — listing technologies without quantifying impact, scope, or technical decisions — make a candidate indistinguishable from a vibe coder. In an era where anyone can claim "I built a full-stack app with AI models," specificity about *what* was done technically (e.g., "fine-tuned" vs. actually modifying model weights) is the differentiator. Weak resume language reads as amateur regardless of the underlying skill level.

### Entry-Level Compression by AI
The video captures an emerging structural reality: tasks historically assigned to junior employees (spreadsheets, boilerplate code, email drafting, simple CRUD features) are increasingly handled by AI tools, eliminating the traditional on-ramp for new graduates. This is not limited to software — it is a white-collar-wide phenomenon. Senior roles persist because they require judgment, context, and accountability that AI does not yet reliably provide.

### Networking as Non-Optional in a Flooded Market
CJ frames networking — specifically direct outreach, Twitter/X DMs, referrals — as the primary hiring channel that still works, citing his own first job obtained via DM to a BitMEX researcher. Applying cold into a resume pile is characterized as lottery-level odds, especially when AI resume screeners are filtering submissions before a human ever sees them. The market's saturation means the social layer of hiring has become load-bearing.

### Unpaid Internships and Leverage Awareness
CJ raises the unpaid internship as an ethical and strategic issue: if a company selected you from 500 applicants, that selection is leverage. Even introverted engineers can and should negotiate for minimum material support (meals, housing stipends). This is positioned as a basic act of professional self-advocacy that many CS students are not taught and do not practice.

### Vibe Coding as Credibility Risk
Beyond resume optics, CJ's implicit concern is that the proliferation of AI-assisted "builders" who cannot explain their own code has devalued the surface-level signals companies used to rely on (projects, tools listed). This creates a credibility arms race where candidates must demonstrate depth — low-level knowledge, architectural decisions, measurable outcomes — to overcome ambient skepticism from hiring teams.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Rewrite resume bullets with technical specificity**: Every bullet should answer what you built, how you built it (specific technical choices), and what it achieved. "Built a full-stack app" is a fail; "Reduced API latency 40% by migrating from REST polling to WebSocket with a custom backpressure implementation" is a pass.
- **Treat networking as your primary job-search channel**: Direct outreach to practitioners (Twitter/X, Discord, GitHub, email) yields far better odds than cold applications. A warm referral bypasses AI resume screeners entirely.
- **Distinguish yourself from AI-generated work**: Be prepared to explain every line, every architectural decision, every trade-off in your projects. If you cannot, neither can your resume.
- **Negotiate unpaid positions immediately**: If selected, use the selection itself as leverage to request at minimum a stipend. Frame it as a professional ask, not a confrontation.
- **Target senior-adjacent roles or specialist niches**: Junior generalist roles face maximum competition and maximum AI displacement pressure. Specialization (systems, quant, security, ML infra) provides a defensible position.
