# The Art of AI Prompting in Finance

## Summary

This webinar, co-hosted by Craig Thompson (Aleph FP&A) and Stephen Hedland (Head of Finance at Relet, an AI-native ERP), surveys how finance and accounting professionals are adopting AI tools in their daily workflows. The session targets a broad audience of FP&A practitioners — from beginners to self-rated 7–8s — and balances high-level landscape discussion with live demonstrations of practical prompting techniques. The central thesis is that finance professionals, historically allergic to non-deterministic outputs, are now finding high-ROI use cases where LLMs add value even without perfect outputs.

The hosts identify January 2025 as an inflection point for AI adoption in finance, driven by CFOs noticing AI spend benefiting other departments (legal, engineering, customer service) while finance lagged behind. They observe that model quality improvements (particularly Claude and Google's latest releases) and AI features embedded directly in tools like Ramp, Brex, and ERPs have accelerated the trend. A key framing throughout: the goal is not to find workflows where AI achieves 100% accuracy, but to identify those where it delivers 95–100% value and reduces the cost of a first draft dramatically.

Specific use cases highlighted include voice-driven idea organization (using ChatGPT's interactive voice mode during commutes), drafting technical accounting memos with rich context loading, and LinkedIn/executive content creation from call transcripts. The session also previews a live demonstration of Claude Projects as a way to provide standing context to an LLM across sessions — a pattern the hosts frame as one of the highest-leverage onramps for finance professionals new to structured AI use.

## Key Concepts

### Context Loading as the Core Prompting Skill

Both hosts repeatedly emphasize that the quality of LLM output scales directly with the richness of context provided. For accounting memos, this means loading the contract, related emails, prior journal entries, and example memos together. Unlike human reviewers who can be overwhelmed by too much information, LLMs perform better with more context. This reframes prompting from "writing a good instruction" to "assembling a complete information package."

### Voice-to-Structured-Output Workflows

Using ChatGPT's interactive voice mode during unstructured time (e.g., a commute) to do stream-of-consciousness idea dumps, then having the model distill those into executive summaries, Slack messages, or emails, is presented as a high-ROI entry point. The workflow separates the generative thinking phase from the structuring phase, leveraging AI for the latter while keeping the human's authentic perspective in the raw input.

### Iterative Editing vs. End-to-End Generation

A recurring principle is that AI output is almost never production-ready, but that is not a reason to avoid the workflow. The hosts describe a pattern where AI gets output to a "good enough starting point" — eliminating writer's block and structural ambiguity — and the human then spends a fraction of the normal time editing rather than drafting from scratch. This is framed as a fundamental mindset shift required to unlock AI value in finance.

### Projects as Persistent Context

The session previews Claude's Projects feature (and equivalent tools in other LLMs) as a mechanism for storing standing context — company information, writing style guides, prior memo examples, chart of accounts — that persists across conversations. This is positioned as a critical step up from ad-hoc prompting, enabling consistent, context-aware outputs without re-loading context each session.

### AI Adoption in Finance: The Parity Inflection Point

The hosts frame the current moment as driven by budget visibility: finance leaders reviewing 2024 spend saw AI tools delivering ROI for engineering (Cursor, Windsurf), legal (Harvey), and customer service teams, and began demanding equivalent tools for finance. This "why not us" dynamic, combined with improving model quality and vendor embedding of AI features, is identified as the structural driver behind the surge in finance AI adoption starting in early 2025.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Load maximum context for accounting memos**: Combine the contract, related emails, prior journal entries, example memos, and a page-length constraint in a single prompt. The output won't be final, but it will compress hours of drafting into minutes of editing.
- **Use voice mode for board prep and executive communication**: Record stream-of-consciousness voice notes during commutes or after meetings, then paste transcripts into an LLM to extract structured insights, talking points, or draft communications. Authentic thinking in → structured output out.
- **Set up a Project (or equivalent) before starting repetitive workflows**: Store company background, tone guidelines, chart of accounts, and example outputs in a persistent project context so every conversation starts informed rather than blank.
- **Calibrate expectations to "first draft acceleration," not "finished output"**: The value metric is time-to-good-draft, not zero-edit output. Expecting perfection leads to abandoning tools that actually deliver strong ROI when paired with human review.
- **Watch for embedded AI features in existing finance tools**: Ramp, Brex, and ERP platforms are shipping native AI agents. Audit current tooling for underutilized AI features before building custom workflows from scratch.

The more context you give as a human, it can tend to kind of like — then we like it too much, right? It becomes too much to review. AI doesn't really have that issue — it actually wants more context because then it gets better."

> "The legacy of the finance professional is to care only about deterministic outcomes, which made us all really allergic to a lot of the initial work that LLMs were doing. And so the framework we really want to apply today is: even in this non-deterministic world, what are some examples of workflows where an LLM can come in and add like 100% value or 98% value — just real ROI."

> "You should never expect it to come out perfect, but that doesn't mean you just stop. That doesn't mean you stop using it — there's still a use case here even if the output isn't perfect from the get-go."
