# 408: The truth behind AI “solving” the Erdos problems

> **Creator**: Mo Bitar | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-02-10 | **Duration**: 10:19

## Summary

Mo Bitar responds to the viral claim that AI is "solving" long-standing Erdős mathematical problems, arguing this narrative misrepresents what LLMs actually do. Drawing on a New Scientist article, he explains that of six Erdős problems reportedly solved by AI as of January 2026, five had already been solved somewhere in the mathematical literature — the AI simply found them. The "breakthrough" is primarily a search and retrieval achievement, not a demonstration of machine reasoning or intelligence.

The deeper point Bitar makes is that LLMs function as extraordinarily capable search engines and synthesizers across vast corpora of human knowledge. They surface connections between papers and ideas that humans couldn't practically find — not because the connections were hidden by complexity, but because the sheer volume of literature made manual discovery impractical. This reframes AI's value: it's not generating novel insight but dramatically reducing the cost of finding existing insight.

Bitar draws direct parallels to software development, noting that what AI is doing for mathematics (clearing low-attention problems from the backlog) mirrors what AI coding tools do for developers. The conclusion is optimistic but grounded: AI amplifies human capacity to tackle previously-neglected problems, and this is likely to increase demand for human intellectual work rather than replace it — a renaissance, not a reckoning.

## Key Concepts

### LLMs as Search Engines, Not Reasoners
The primary mechanism behind AI "solving" Erdős problems was tool use and literature retrieval — finding relevant papers that humans hadn't cross-referenced. Bitar argues LLMs form statistical relationships between documents in a non-trivial way, but this is categorically different from understanding or creative problem-solving. The calculator analogy: output is meaningful to us, not to the system producing it.

### The Attention Problem in Knowledge Work
Many problems in both mathematics and software engineering go unsolved not because they're intractable but because skilled people focus their limited time on higher-ROI work. LLMs are tireless and can be directed at low-attention problems indefinitely — this is their structural advantage, not superior reasoning. Five of six "solved" Erdős problems were already solved; they just hadn't been found.

### Information Indexing at Scale
A recurring insight in the video is that humanity has accumulated far more knowledge than its indexing systems can surface. Google fails at scale for deep semantic retrieval; LLMs succeed because they've internalized the corpus and can traverse it along semantic dimensions. This is positioned as the "true use" of LLMs — making the existing sum of human knowledge practically accessible rather than nominally accessible.

### Human Amplifier vs. Human Replacer
Bitar explicitly frames AI tools as amplifiers: they unblock humans from low-level friction (finding a relevant paper, fixing a boring bug) so human effort can concentrate on harder, more creative problems. Empirically, he notes developers are working harder and longer because they're no longer blocked — productivity is unlocked, not displaced. The backlog shrinks; the frontier expands.

### Hype Narrative Inversion
The viral framing — "AI solves unsolvable math problems" — inverts the actual story. Recognizing this pattern (hype outrunning reality, as with the Claudebot incident Bitar references) is practical signal-filtering for anyone trying to assess AI capability claims accurately.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Treat AI capability claims skeptically**: Before accepting "AI solved X," ask whether it generated something novel or retrieved something existing. The distinction matters enormously for assessing real progress.
- **Use LLMs for literature and reference search**: The strongest demonstrated use case here is cross-domain knowledge retrieval — feeding an LLM a problem and asking it to surface relevant prior work is high-value and well-validated.
- **Reframe your backlog**: Low-attention, low-glamour tasks in your domain are now tractable. LLMs work best on problems that are well-defined but time-consuming — use this deliberately rather than waiting for it to happen.
- **Don't mistake tirelessness for intelligence**: The value of an LLM spending "24 hours" on a paper is real, but it's operational value (scale, persistence), not cognitive value (insight, judgment). Design workflows accordingly.
- **Expect increased workload, not decreased**: The evidence from both math and software is that AI tools increase throughput and raise the ceiling of what teams attempt — plan for more work at higher complexity, not the same work with fewer people.
