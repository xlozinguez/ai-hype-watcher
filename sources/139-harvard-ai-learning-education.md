# 139: Harvard Thinking: Preserving learning in the age of AI shortcuts

> **Creator**: Harvard University | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2026-02-19 | **Duration**: 28:23

## Summary

A panel discussion from the Harvard Thinking podcast featuring Michael Brenner (applied mathematics, also a research scientist at Google studying LLMs for science), Tina Grotzer (cognitive scientist at the Graduate School of Education), and Ying Shu (assistant professor studying AI technologies for children's development). The conversation explores how generative AI tools affect learning, critical thinking, and the purpose of education — from elementary through graduate level.

The panelists navigate the tension between AI as a productivity multiplier and AI as a threat to foundational skill development. A survey of 7,000 high school students reveals that nearly half feel they rely on AI too much, and over 40% tried to limit usage but failed. The discussion converges on metacognition — understanding how your own mind works compared to AI — as a crucial new purpose for education, alongside preserving the social-emotional dimensions of learning that AI cannot replicate.

## Key Concepts

### The Two Dimensions of Learning at Risk

Learning operates along two dimensions: what students learn (facts and information) and their ability to learn (foundational capacities like critical thinking, self-regulation, and metacognition). AI tools threaten the second dimension most acutely — when homework that once required hours of practice and comprehension is completed in minutes, the cognitive development that came from the struggle is lost.

### Metacognition as a New Educational Purpose

Tina Grotzer advocates for metacognition — understanding how human embodied minds differ from AI — as a central goal of education. Students who understand what their minds do better than AI can make informed decisions about when to delegate tasks versus when to engage cognitively. This "Venn diagram" approach — mapping where human and AI capabilities overlap and diverge — gives students agency rather than dependence.

### The Irreplaceable Social-Emotional Component

Research shows that while AI tutors can match human tutors on information retention, students report higher enjoyment and are more engaged with human tutors. In an experiment, students rated identical essay feedback as significantly more useful when told it came from an instructor versus AI. Expert human tutors manage motivation, social-emotional context, and escalate support in ways AI cannot replicate — they know when to withhold information, let a student struggle, or provide encouragement.

### Raising the Bar Instead of Lowering It

Michael Brenner responded to discovering that Gemini could solve his graduate-level problem sets by completely redesigning his course. Instead of traditional problem sets, students had to invent problems that chatbots could not solve and verify solutions through peer review and numerical calculations. This produced 600 novel problems and deeper learning — students knew more than any previous cohort because they were forced to push beyond what AI could do.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Use AI to raise difficulty, not reduce it**: When AI can solve standard problems, redesign assignments to require invention, synthesis, and pushing beyond AI capabilities.
- **Measure actual learning outcomes**: Experiment with AI tools but verify through assessment (oral exams, demonstrations) that students are genuinely learning more, not just producing more output.
- **Teach self-regulation alongside AI use**: Students need the metacognitive skill of planning which parts of a task they will think through themselves versus delegate to AI.
- **Preserve human relationships in education**: Restructure schools to enable teachers to know students deeply — the social-emotional holding environment is what AI cannot replace.
- **Age-appropriate introduction**: Specialized AI tools (phonics, math) can work for young children, but general-purpose AI assistants require cognitive self-regulation skills that take time to develop.
