# Can't We Just… Write Code?

## Summary

Lama Dev opens a planned three-part series by diagnosing the malaise affecting many developers today: AI chaos, technology churn fatigue, and social media pressure. After months of stepping back from content creation to observe developers, freelancers, and juniors, the creator concludes that the despair is real but largely driven by distorted signals — hype cycles, influencer noise, and the firehose of new tooling — rather than by any fundamental collapse of the developer's role.

The core argument is historical and structural: web development has always evolved in waves (tables → CSS → JavaScript frameworks → full-stack → cloud/DevOps → AI), and each wave follows the same pattern. As tools get easier, expectations rise to fill the reclaimed time, and the developers who had strong fundamentals adapted fastest every single time. That pattern, the creator argues, is not changing — it's just running faster now, which makes it feel unprecedented when it isn't.

The practical prescription distills to five rules: build strong fundamentals before chasing tools, accept that no one knows everything (seniors are fast because they know *where to look*, not because they've memorized more), resist the pressure to use the latest technology (stable beats trendy for real production work), stop comparing yourself to social-media highlight reels, and replace passive content consumption with hands-on small projects. The next video in the series promises a grounded assessment of AI in programming specifically.

## Key Concepts

### The Repeating Adoption Pattern in Web Development
Every major technology transition — table layouts to CSS, CSS to JavaScript, JS to frameworks, frameworks to full-stack, on-premise to cloud — followed the same curve: tooling gets easier, which raises the floor for what is expected of a developer. In each case, developers who already had adjacent fundamentals (e.g., designers who already knew HTML) adapted quickly, while those who had skipped fundamentals in favor of the hot tool struggled. This is not a new crisis; it is an old loop running at higher speed.

### Fundamentals as Durable Competitive Advantage
The creator explicitly frames the current AI moment as another iteration of this pattern. The competition is not "uses AI" vs. "doesn't use AI" — it is "prompts and hopes" vs. "understands what the AI is doing and uses it strategically." Developers with strong core knowledge (JavaScript mechanics, browser behavior, data structures, API design) will leverage AI more effectively than those who learned to prompt without understanding the output. This is the same advantage that HTML/CSS knowledge gave web designers when JavaScript emerged.

### The Reliability Preference of Production Systems
Despite the hype around new frameworks and libraries, most companies running real systems optimize for stability, not novelty. ExpressJS pulling 7 million weekly downloads is cited as evidence that the industry's actual workload runs on proven, boring infrastructure. The gap between what is hyped on social media and what is deployed in production is wide, and developers who mistake the former for the latter will over-invest in learning tools that never reach their day job.

### Social Media as a Distorted Signal
The creator distinguishes the old developer community (domain experts discussing craft) from the current landscape (everyone is an influencer, bots are prevalent, fake success stories dominate). Treating social media feeds as a proxy for "what I need to know" creates chronic anxiety and misallocates learning time. The recommended reframe: compare yourself to who you were six months ago, run a 15-minute weekly self-review (what did I build, what did I learn, what's next), and treat feed content as noise rather than signal.

### Vibe Coding and the Limits of Pure Prompting
Though not named explicitly as "vibe coding," the creator acknowledges testing it ("I even tried vibe coding to see if it was a real thing") as part of their observation period. The implicit verdict: using AI without understanding the output is the modern equivalent of copying tutorial code without understanding state, effects, or rendering — it works until it doesn't, and debugging is painful without fundamentals. The framing anticipates the series' next video, which will address AI tooling directly.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Invest in fundamentals before tools.** The JavaScript core knowledge series the creator references is free at llama.dev; more broadly, the principle is that any hour spent on fundamentals compounds across multiple tool transitions while hours spent on a specific hyped tool may not.
- **Calibrate "falling behind" correctly.** Seniors are not faster because they know more facts; they are faster because pattern recognition tells them where to look. You don't need to have memorized every API — you need to know how to evaluate and apply what you find.
- **Bias toward stable, proven technology for production work.** Unless you're at a startup greenfielding a new system, the correct default is battle-tested libraries and frameworks, not whatever was announced last month.
- **Replace passive research with active experimentation.** Hours spent reading "framework A vs. framework B" debates are usually less informative than a small project that forces a direct comparison with your actual requirements.
- **Weekly self-review as an antidote to social comparison.** A brief, structured reflection (what did I create? what did I learn? what's next?) anchors progress to personal trajectory rather than the highlight reel of others.
