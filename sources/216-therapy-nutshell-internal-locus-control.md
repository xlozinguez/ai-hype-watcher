# 216: How to Build an Internal Locus of Control

> **Creator**: Therapy in a Nutshell | **Platform**: YouTube | **Date**: 2025-04-10 | **Duration**: 20:13

## Summary

Therapist Emma McAdam explains the concept of locus of control — the degree to which you believe you can influence what happens in your life — and provides a practical framework for shifting from external to internal locus of control. While not directly about AI, this psychological concept is deeply relevant to how developers and knowledge workers navigate the AI transition: whether they feel empowered to adapt and learn new skills, or helpless in the face of disruption.

The video uses the story of Ben Carson's upbringing to illustrate how an internal locus of control — focusing energy on what you can change while accepting what you cannot — leads to resilience and success even in the face of systemic challenges. The framework maps directly to the mindset required for professionals adapting to AI-augmented workflows.

## Key Concepts

### Internal vs. External Locus of Control

People with an internal locus of control believe their efforts make a difference and focus energy on what they can change. Those with an external locus believe luck, fate, or other people's actions determine outcomes. Internal locus is linked to better physical health, mental health, relationships, and professional success. Crucially, this is a learnable thinking pattern, not a fixed trait.

### The Helplessness Wobble

External locus of control manifests in two forms that people oscillate between: externalizing (blaming circumstances, other people, or systems) and internalizing (labeling yourself as defective, too stupid, or too broken to succeed). Both forms are disempowering — they provide vindication but eliminate agency. Recognizing this pattern is the first step to breaking it.

### Four Steps to Internal Locus of Control

1. **Identify self-limiting beliefs**: Fill in "I can't ___ because ___" to surface where you feel powerless.
2. **Clarify what you can and cannot control**: Draw two columns and honestly sort your situation into controllable and uncontrollable factors.
3. **Explore unknown skills**: Ask "what skills might I not even know about?" — successful people get curious about what they don't know yet rather than wallowing in limitations.
4. **Take action on one thing**: Choose one item from the "can control" column and act on it, however small.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Apply to AI career anxiety**: Instead of "AI will replace my job" (external), ask "What skills can I develop to work effectively with AI?" (internal locus).
- **Labels as explanations, not excuses**: Using a limitation (ADHD, background, skill gap) as an explanation means seeking support and accommodations; using it as an excuse means giving up.
- **Focus energy on the controllable**: Healthy, successful people are not optimistic about everything — they are skilled at identifying the narrow band of things they can actually influence and directing all energy there.
- **Curiosity as an antidote to helplessness**: When feeling stuck, the question "What skills don't I know about yet?" opens pathways that victimhood thinking closes.

## Notable Quotes

> "The difference between people who feel empowered and hopeful and get stuff done and those who feel frustrated and helpless — it's how to develop an internal locus of control." — Emma McAdam
