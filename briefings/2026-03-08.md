## Details

### Scouts vs. Strike Teams: The AI-Era Organizational Model

Nate B Jones ([250](../sources/250-nate-b-jones-team-size-ai.md)) provides the most actionable organizational framework to emerge from this batch: when AI multiplies per-person output by 5-10x, coordination cost scales by the same factor, making teams >5 people catastrophically expensive. The solution bifurcates into two archetypes:

**Scouts** — Solo operators with full AI toolkits for exploration. Mission: map territory, test viability, prototype. Exemplar: Peter Steinberger building OpenClaw in 60 days (one person, 20 years of judgment, swarm of agents). Test readiness: give someone a real problem, full AI tooling, one week, zero check-ins.

**Strike Teams** — Five-person production units where every AI-generated output passes through at least one other brain with shared context. Why five? Human cognitive limit (Dunbar's research), communication pathway scaling (5 people = 10 pathways, 10 = 45, 20 = 190), and coordination cost amplification (at $2M/year per AI-augmented person, the sixth member costs millions in lost productivity).

**The provocation**: Companies framing AI as a cost story ("same work, fewer people") are missing the opportunity. A 500-person company didn't get 20% headcount reduction — it got the productive capacity of 2,500-5,000 people. The right question: what was previously impossible when each 5-person team has the capacity of a 50-person department?

**Source**: [250: Your Team is Probably Too Big](../sources/250-nate-b-jones-team-size-ai.md)

### The AI Honeymoon Is Over: Maturity, Fatigue, and the Hype Gap

Two sources capture the transition from breathless excitement to mature pragmatism. Daniel and Hunter from TMBSA ([246](../sources/246-the-blur-ai-honeymoon-over.md)) frame it as the "iPhone upgrade analogy" — Opus 4.5 to 4.6 is meaningfully better but doesn't warrant dropping everything to switch. Enterprise model upgrades now happen on deprecation cycles, not hype cycles. The gap between social media discourse ("everything has changed!") and practitioner reality (incremental gains, significant friction, realistic expectations) is widening.

Matteo Cassese ([252](../sources/252-matteo-cassese-agentic-fever.md)) identifies **agentic fever** — FOMO that every moment not spent running agents is wasted time. Silicon Valley collectively experiencing "some people think it's already over, last few months to make money." The paradox: frantic, anxiety-driven prompting produces frantic, failing agents. Good inputs → good outputs. His recovery prescription: recognize you're on the bleeding edge (not falling behind), return to healthy routines, shift from builder to instructor (language is now code, markdown SOPs are development).

**Key insight**: The era of "you are an expert X" system prompts is over. Modern frontier models don't need persona instructions — they need institutional context (database schemas, internal terminology, customer specifics). Skills files should contain novel knowledge the model lacks, not generic expertise.

**Sources**: [246: The AI Honeymoon Is Over](../sources/246-the-blur-ai-honeymoon-over.md), [252: Agentic Fever](../sources/252-matteo-cassese-agentic-fever.md)

### The C++ as Assembly Fallacy: Why You Still Need to Read AI Code

Shahesh (45 years programming experience, [251](../sources/251-less-assembly-required-ai-replace-programmers.md)) systematically dismantles the "treat AI code like compiler output" argument. The analogy fails on three properties:

1. **Compiler output is deterministic**; AI output is non-deterministic (same prompts → different programs)
2. **Source code fully specifies compiler output**; prompts do not fully specify AI output
3. **When modifying compiled programs, you change source and recompile**; when modifying AI code, the agent reverse-engineers intent from the code itself (prompts aren't source code)

The **context window cliff**: when a codebase grows beyond the AI's effective context window, performance doesn't degrade gradually — it drops sharply. Some agents stop and report limitation. Others hallucinate silently: code compiles, passes tests, appears to function, but is semantically wrong. The hallucinated code "is optimized to look good even when it's meaningless."

**Implication for scouts vs. strike teams**: Scouts can prototype quickly, but must hand off to strike teams before the context cliff. Strike teams need humans to refactor AI-generated code into maintainable abstractions. The "2x productivity gain" cited by veteran programmers is bottlenecked by human review time — not 10x, "even 2x is a stretch."

**Source**: [251: Can AI replace us?](../sources/251-less-assembly-required-ai-replace-programmers.md)

### Enterprise Adoption: The Reality Gap

[243: SKILLS Legal AI Playbooks](../sources/243-skills-legal-ai-playbooks.md) provides hard numbers on enterprise adoption velocity. Legal AI playbook generation market (perfect use case: knowledge work, standardized contracts, clear ROI) shows Harvey at 26.2% adoption among law firms with a long tail of single-digit competitors. Market is early and fragmented — no vendor lock-in yet.

**Customer reality check**: Even in domains perfectly suited for AI, adoption is <30% and the market hasn't converged on winners. Corporate legal departments have used playbooks for decades; law firms are just discovering AI makes playbooks viable for diverse client work.

**Security as friction** ([244](../sources/244-agenticflow-openclaw-security.md), [249](../sources/249-ai-security-ops-claude-cowork.md)): OpenClaw stores credentials in plain text, no sandboxing. Claude Cowork shows 17.8% prompt injection rate, logging gaps. Enterprises cannot adopt tools that fail basic security posture, regardless of productivity gains. Scouts can run OpenClaw on isolated machines; strike teams shipping to customers need SOC 2, audit logs, role-based access control.

**Sources**: [243: SKILLS Legal AI Playbooks](../sources/243-skills-legal-ai-playbooks.md), [244: OpenClaw Security Audit](../sources/244-agenticflow-openclaw-security.md), [249: Claude Cowork Security](../sources/249-ai-security-ops-claude-cowork.md)
