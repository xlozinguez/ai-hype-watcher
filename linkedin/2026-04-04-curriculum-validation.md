```
We validated 522 AI engineering concepts against external evidence. 65% held up. The other 35% taught us more.

Over the past six months, I built a structured curriculum covering AI-assisted development across six modules: foundations, prompting, tooling, agentic patterns, team orchestration, strategy. 348+ source notes from 100+ creators. Then we stress-tested every single concept against independent research, benchmarks, and market data.

What turned out to be real: spec-first development graduated from "good advice" to dedicated tooling (AWS Kiro, GitHub Spec Kit). Context engineering became the consensus discipline, with Gartner recommending orgs appoint context engineering leads. Harness engineering got a formal taxonomy from Martin Fowler and an arxiv paper. Skills files became an open standard adopted by OpenAI, Google, and GitHub.

What we were overblowing: the 97.5% agent failure headline. External benchmarks show a spectrum (75-97.5%) depending on task structure. The SaaSpocalypse partially reversed as companies pivoted to outcome-based pricing. CLI-only absolutism softened as MCP schema filtering closed 90% of the efficiency gap.

The number that should concern everyone: 3.3% paid Copilot adoption (Forrester), 90% of firms reporting zero AI productivity impact (NBER). Models now handle 14+ hours of human expert effort on benchmarks. The capability is real. The enterprise value capture is not.

The biggest surprise: power, not GPUs, is now the binding infrastructure constraint. Microsoft is sitting on $80B in unfulfilled Azure orders because it can't get electricity to run GPUs it already owns. Hyperscaler capex hit $660-690B, consuming nearly 100% of operating cash flow. That's up from a 10-year average of 40%.

Our skeptical posture was vindicated more often than it was wrong. 55% of concepts confirmed outright. Only 6% needed corrections. The real lesson: being grounded about AI isn't pessimism. It's the only posture that lets you invest in what actually works.

If you're building with AI on Monday morning, here's what matters: spec your intent before you prompt, engineer your context like infrastructure, and stop trusting demos that skip the production gap.

What concept in your AI workflow has held up to scrutiny vs. one that quietly stopped working?
```
